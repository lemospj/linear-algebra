    Section 2.7 Eigenvalues and eigenvectors

    Consider the linear map associated to the matrix \(A=\begin{pmatrix} \frac{1}{2} \amp \frac{1}{2} \\ \frac{1}{2} \amp \frac{1}{2} \end{pmatrix}\text{.}\) You may not realize it immediately, but this linear map is precisely the projection onto the line \(y=x\text{.}\) As you know, the projection of any vector already on the line will be itself. For example,
    \begin{equation*} A\colvec{1 \\ 1} = \colvec{1 \\ 1}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    However, if \(\bv\) is a vector that is orthogonal to the line, \(A\bv=\bz\text{.}\) For instance, the vector \(\colvec{1 \\ -1}\) is orthogonal to every vector in the line \(y=x\text{,}\) and so
    \begin{equation*} A\colvec{1 \\ -1}=\colvec{0 \\ 0}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    These two vectors are examples of eigenvectors of \(A\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Definition 2.7.1.

    An eigenvector of a linear map \(f:\R^n\rightarrow \R^n\) (or of the associated matrix) is a non-zero vector \(\bv\in\R^n\) such that \(f(\bv)=\lambda\bv\) for some scalar \(\lambda\in\R\text{.}\) In this situation, we say that \(\lambda\) is the associated eigenvalue (which may be \(0\)).
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.7.2.

    In the example above where \(A=\begin{pmatrix}\frac{1}{2} \amp \frac{1}{2} \\ \frac{1}{2} \amp \frac{1}{2} \end{pmatrix}\text{,}\) the vector \(\colvec{1 \\ 1}\) is an eigenvector associated to the eigenvalue \(1\text{,}\) while the vector \(\colvec{1 \\ -1}\) is an eigenvector associated to the eigenvalue \(0\text{.}\) So \(1\) and \(0\) are eigenvalues of \(A\text{.}\) They turn out to be all the eigenvalues of \(A\text{.}\) (We will soon see how this can be shown.) What other eigenvectors are there associated to \(0\text{?}\) To answer this question we must solve the system
    \begin{equation*} A\colvec{x \\ y} = \colvec{0 \\ 0}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    It can be easily see that the solutions to this system are all the vectors of the form \(\colvec{x \\ -x}\text{.}\) This means that the eigenvectors associated to \(0\) are precisely the non-zero vectors of the form \(\colvec{x \\ -x}\text{.}\) This is precisely the orthogonal space to the line \(y=x\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Let’s now try to find all the vectors associated to the eigenvalue \(1\text{.}\) This boils down to solving the system
    \begin{equation*} A\colvec{x \\ y}=\colvec{x \\ y}\text{,} \end{equation*}
    or, equivalently, the system
    \begin{equation*} (A-\Id_2)\colvec{x \\ y}=\colvec{0 \\ 0}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    It can be easily see that all the solutions to this system are the vectors of the form \(\colvec{x \\ x}\text{,}\) i.e. all the vectors in the line \(y=x\text{.}\) The eigenvectors associated to the eigenvalue \(1\) are then the non-zero vectors in the line \(y=x\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.7.3.

    Consider the matrix \(A=\begin{pmatrix} 2 \amp 0 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 2 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 3\end{pmatrix}\text{.}\) The eigenvalues of \(A\) are \(2\) and \(3\) (take this for granted for now). Let’s try to describe all eigenvectors associated to the eigenvalue \(2\text{.}\) This means that we want to solve the system
    \begin{equation*} A\colvec{x \\ y \\ z}=2\colvec{x \\ y \\ z}\text{,} \end{equation*}
    which is equivalent to the system
    \begin{equation*} (A-2\Id_2)\colvec{x \\ y \\ z}= \colvec{0 \\ 0 \\ 0}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Now, \(A-2\Id_3=\begin{pmatrix} 0 \amp 0 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 0\amp 1\end{pmatrix}\text{.}\) The solutions to
    \begin{equation*} \begin{pmatrix} 0 \amp 0 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 0\amp 1\end{pmatrix}\colvec{x \\ y \\ z}=\colvec{0 \\ 0 \\ 0} \end{equation*}
    are all the vectors of the form \(\colvec{x \\ y \\ 0}\text{.}\) So, the eigenvectors associated to \(2\) are all the non-zero vectors in \(\colvec{x \\ y \\0}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Similarly, we may calculate the eigenvectors associated to the eigenvalue \(3\text{.}\) These are the non-zero solutions to
    \begin{equation*} (A-3\Id_3)\colvec{x \\ y \\ z} = \colvec{0 \\ 0 \\ 0}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    The solutions to this system are the vectors of the form \(\colvec{0 \\ 0 \\ z}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    If \(A\) is an \(n\times n\) matrix, and if \(\lambda\) is an eigenvalue of \(A\text{,}\) then the eigenvectors associated to \(\lambda\) are the non-zero vectors in \(\R^n\) that satisfy
    \begin{equation*} A\bv=\lambda\bv\text{.} \end{equation*}
    These are also the vectors that satisfy
    \begin{equation*} (A-\lambda\Id_n)\bv=\bz\text{.} \end{equation*}
    So, the eigenvectors of \(A\) associated to \(\lambda\) are precisely the non-zero vectors in the kernel of \(A-\lambda\Id_n\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Definition 2.7.4.

    If \(f:\R^n\rightarrow\R^n\) is a linear map, and if \(\lambda\) is an eigenvalue of \(f\text{,}\) then the eigenspace associated to the eigenvalue \(\lambda\) is the kernel of \(f-\lambda\Id_n\text{.}\)
    🔗
    We can write this in terms of matrices. If \(A\) is an \(n\times n\) matrix, then the eigenspace associated to the eigenvalue \(\lambda\) is the kernel of \(A-\lambda\Id_n\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.7.5.

    In example Example 2.7.2, we see that the eigenspace associated to \(1\) is the kernel of
    \begin{equation*} \begin{pmatrix} \frac{1}{2} \amp \frac{1}{2} \\ \frac{1}{2} \amp \frac{1}{2}\end{pmatrix}-\begin{pmatrix} 1 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 1\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix} -\frac{1}{2} \amp \frac{1}{2} \\ \frac{1}{2} \amp -\frac{1}{2}\end{pmatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    We already found that this is precisely the linear subspace generated by \(\colvec{1 \\ 1}\text{.}\) In particular, it has dimension \(1\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Similarly, the eigenspace associated to \(0\text{,}\) is generated by \(\colvec{1 \\ -1}\) and also has dimension \(1\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.7.6.

    In Example 2.7.3, the eigenspace associated to \(2\) has dimension \(2\text{,}\) while the eigenspace associated to \(3\) has dimension \(1\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    At the moment, given a square matrix \(A\) and an eigenvalue \(\lambda\) of \(A\text{,}\) we know how to determine the eigenspace associated to \(\lambda\text{.}\) However, we still do not know how to find the eigenvalues of \(A\) in the first place. To be able to do this, we will need to speak about the characteristic polynomial of a square matrix \(A\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Definition 2.7.7.

    Let \(A\) be an \(n\times n\) matrix. The characteristic polynomial of \(A\) is the polynomial
    \begin{equation*} p_A(x)=\det(x\Id_n-A)\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.7.8.

    Let’s consider the matrix \(A=\begin{pmatrix} 2 \amp -1 \\ 1 \amp 3\end{pmatrix}\text{.}\) In order to calculate its characteristic polynomial, we will need to work out the expression for \(x\Id_2-A\text{.}\) But this is just
    \begin{equation*} x\Id_2-A=\begin{pmatrix} x \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp x\end{pmatrix}-\begin{pmatrix}2 \amp -1 \\ 1 \amp 3\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}x-2 \amp 1 \\ -1 \amp x-3\end{pmatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    The determinant of this matrix is \((x-2)(x-3)+1=x^2-5x+7\text{,}\) so we conclude that the characteristic polynomial of \(A\) is \(x^2-5x+7\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.7.9.

    Going back to the matrix in Example 2.7.2, we have
    \begin{equation*} x\Id_2-A = \begin{pmatrix}x-\frac{1}{2} \amp -\frac{1}{2} \\ -\frac{1}{2} \amp x-\frac{1}{2}\end{pmatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Therefore, the characteristic polynomial of \(A\) is
    \begin{equation*} p_A(x)=\left(x-\frac{1}{2}\right)^2-\frac{1}{4}=x^2-x=x(x-1)\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Note that the zeros of this polynomial are \(0\) and \(1\text{.}\) These are precisely the eigenvalues of \(A\text{.}\) This is not a coincidence!
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.7.10.

    Let’s now consider the matrix \(A=\begin{pmatrix} 2 \amp 0 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 2 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 3\end{pmatrix}\) from Example 2.7.3. We have
    \begin{equation*} x\Id_3-A=\begin{pmatrix}x-2 \amp 0 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp x-2 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 0 \amp x-3\end{pmatrix}\text{,} \end{equation*}
    and so the characteristic polynomial of \(A\) is \((x-2)^2(x-3)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    In the next section, we will show the important result that the eigenvalues of a linear map \(f:\R^n\rightarrow\R^n\) are precisely the zeros of its characteristic polynomial. For now, let’s simply apply this fact.
    🔗

    Example 2.7.11.

    Consider the matrix \(A=\begin{pmatrix} \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2} \amp -\frac{1}{2} \\ \frac{1}{2} \amp \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}\end{pmatrix}\text{.}\) This is the matrix that corresponds to a rotation of \(\R^2\) by an angle of \(\pi/6\) radians around the origin. As such, we should expect that there are no eigenvectors, and so no eigenvalues. Let’s confirm that this is the case using the characteristic polynomial.
    🔗
    Since
    \begin{equation*} x\Id_2-A=\begin{pmatrix} x-\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2} \amp \frac{1}{2} \\ -\frac{1}{2} \amp x-\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}\end{pmatrix}\text{,} \end{equation*}
    we see that the characteristic polynomial of \(A\) is given by
    \begin{equation*} p_A(x) = \left(x-\frac{\sqrt{3}}{4}\right)^2+\frac{1}{4}=x^2-\sqrt{3}x+1\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Since the discriminant of this polynomial is \(-1\text{,}\) we conclude that it has no real roots. Therefore, \(A\) does not have real eigenvalues.
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.7.12.

    Consider the matrix \(A=\begin{pmatrix} 0 \amp 1 \\ 1 \amp 0\end{pmatrix}\text{.}\) This is the matrix that corresponds to reflection along the line \(y=x\) in \(\R^2\text{.}\) Geometrically, one would guess that there are two eigenvalues. One of them should be \(1\) because every vector on the line \(y=x\) will be sent to itself. The other should be \(-1\) because every vector that is orthogonal to the line \(y=x\) should be sent to its opposite. Let’s confirm that this is the case.
    🔗
    The characteristic polynomial of \(A\) is
    \begin{equation*} p_A(x)=\det\begin{pmatrix} x \amp -1 \\ -1 \amp x \end{pmatrix} = x^2-1-(x-1)(x+1)\text{.} \end{equation*}
    So, indeed, the two roots of \(p_A\) are \(1\) and \(-1\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Now that we have the eigenvalues, we may try to find the corresponding eigenvectors. The eigenspace corresponding to \(1\) is the kernel of \(A-\Id_2=\begin{pmatrix}-1 \amp 1 \\ 1 \amp -1\end{pmatrix}\text{.}\) We can quickly solve the associated linear system to conclude that the eigenspace associated to \(1\) is precisely the set of vectors of the form \(\colvec{s \\ s}\) for some real number \(s\text{.}\) In other words, it is precisely the line \(y=x\) along which we are performing our reflection.
    🔗
    The eigenspace associated to \(-1\) is the kernel of \(A+\Id_2=\begin{pmatrix} 1\amp 1 \\ 1 \amp 1\end{pmatrix}\text{.}\) Solving the system, we conclude that it is the linear subspace consisting of the vectors of the form \(\colvec{s \\ -s}\) for some \(s\in\R\text{.}\) In other words, it is precisely the line \(y=-x\) which is orthogonal to \(y=x\) and passes through the origin.
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.7.13.

    Let’s now consider the matrix \(A=\begin{pmatrix}\frac{1}{2} \amp -\frac{1}{2} \amp 0 \\ -\frac{1}{2} \amp \frac{1}{2} \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 1\end{pmatrix}\text{.}\) You can check that this is the matrix corresponding to the projection of \(\R^3\) onto the linear subspace given by \(x+y=0\text{.}\) Once again, we can guess that we should have two eigenvalues: \(0\) and \(1\text{.}\) The eigenspace corresponding to \(0\) should be the orthogonal complement to \(V\text{,}\) while \(V\) should be the eigenspace corresponding to \(1\text{.}\) Let’s confirm that all of this holds.
    🔗
    The characteristic polynomial is
    \begin{align*} p_A(x)\amp =\det\begin{pmatrix} x-\frac{1}{2} \amp \frac{1}{2} \amp 0 \\ \frac{1}{2} \amp x-\frac{1}{2} \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 0 \amp x-1\end{pmatrix} \\ \amp = \left(x-\frac{1}{2}\right)^2(x-1)-\frac{1}{4}(x-1)\\ \amp =x(x-1)^2\text{.} \end{align*}
    The zeros of this polynomial are \(0\) and \(1\text{,}\) so these are the eigenvalues of \(A\text{.}\) Let’s calculate the corresponding eigenspaces.
    🔗
    We need to find the kernels of \(A\) and of \(A-\Id_3\text{.}\) Both of these can be quickly calculated. The kernel of \(A\) is the subspace of \(\R^3\) spanned by \(\colvec{1 \\ 1\\ 0}\text{,}\) which is precisely \(V^{\perp}\text{,}\) while the kernel of \(A-\Id_3\) is precisely \(V\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
