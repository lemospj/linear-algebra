    Section 2.9 Change of basis

    The objective of this chapter is to learn how to do linear algebra calculations over different bases. The first question we will be interested in is that of writing a given vector as a linear combination of the vectors in a given basis. We already know how to do this because this is essentially the problem of solving a system of linear equations, but we can streamline the process using inverse matrices.
    🔗
    Say that we have a basis \(B=(\bv_1,\bv_2,\ldots,\bv_n)\) of \(\R^n\) (the order of the vectors in the basis is important!) and a vector \(\bw\text{,}\) also in \(\R^n\text{.}\) Our goal is to write \(\bw\) as a linear combination of the vectors \(\bv_1,\bv_2,\ldots,\bv_n\text{.}\) In other words, we want to find scalars \(x_1,x_2,\ldots, x_n\) such that
    \begin{equation*} \bw=x_1\bv_1+x_2\bv_2+\ldots+x_n\bv_n\text{.} \end{equation*}
    If \(P\) is the matrix whose columns are the vectors \(\bv_1,\bv_2,\ldots,\bv_n\text{,}\) then this is equivalent to solving the system
    \begin{equation*} \bw=P\colvec{x_1 \\ x_2 \\ \vdots \\ x_n}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    The matrix \(P\) is called the transition matrix from the standard unit basis to the basis \(B\text{.}\) Since the columns are linearly independent vectors in \(\R^n\text{,}\) this matrix is invertible, and so
    \begin{equation*} \colvec{x_1 \\ x_2 \\ \vdots \\ x_n}=P^{-1}\bw\text{.} \end{equation*}
    The coefficients \(x_1,\ldots, x_n\) above are called the coordinates of the vector \(\bw\) in the basis \(B\text{.}\) Because a single vector will have different coordinates depending on which basis we are working, it will be useful to use some sort of notation to keep remind us of the basis. We will write \([\bw]_B\) for the representation of \(\bw\) in the basis \(B\text{,}\) i.e.
    \begin{equation*} [\bw]_B=\colvec{x_1 \\ x_2 \\ \vdots \\ x_n}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗

    Example 2.9.1.

    Consider the basis \(\colvec{1 \\ 2}, \colvec{3 \\ -1}\) of \(\R^2\text{.}\) Call it \(B\text{.}\) The transition matrix is \(P=\begin{pmatrix}1 \amp 3 \\ 2 \amp -1\end{pmatrix}\text{.}\) Its inverse is
    \begin{equation*} P^{-1}=\frac{1}{7}\begin{pmatrix} 1 \amp 3 \\ 2 \amp -1\end{pmatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Therefore, the vector \(\bw=\colvec{-1 \\ 2}\) in the basis \(B\) has coordinates
    \begin{equation*} [\bw]_B = P^{-1}\colvec{-1 \\ 2} = \colvec{5/7 \\ -4/7}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Recall that what this means is that \(\bw = \frac{5}{7}\colvec{1 \\ 2} -\frac{4}{7}\colvec{3 \\ -1}\text{.}\) If you work the right-hand side out, you can indeed verify that this is true.
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.9.2.

    Now let \(B\) be the basis \(\colvec{1 \\ 0 \\ -1},\colvec{2 \\ 1 \\ 0}, \colvec{-1 \\ 1 \\ 1}\) of \(\R^3\text{.}\) Let’s try to write the vector \(\bw=\colvec{2 \\ 2 \\ -3}\) in this basis.
    🔗
    The transition matrix in this case is \(P=\begin{pmatrix} 1 \amp 2 \amp -1 \\ 0 \amp 1 \amp 1 \\ -1 \amp 0 \amp 1 \end{pmatrix}\text{.}\) We need to calculate the inverse \(P^{-1}\text{.}\)
    \begin{align*} \left(\begin{array}{ccc|ccc} 1 \amp 2 \amp -1 \amp 1 \amp 0 \amp 0\\ 0 \amp 1 \amp 1 \amp 0 \amp 1 \amp 0\\ -1 \amp 0 \amp 1 \amp 0 \amp 0 \amp 1 \end{array}\right) \amp \xrightarrow{R_3\to R_3+R1} \left(\begin{array}{ccc|ccc} 1 \amp 2 \amp -1 \amp 1 \amp 0 \amp 0\\ 0 \amp 1 \amp 1 \amp 0 \amp 1 \amp 0\\ 0 \amp 2 \amp 0 \amp 1 \amp 0 \amp 1 \end{array}\right)\\ \amp \xrightarrow{R_3\to R_3-2R_2} \left(\begin{array}{ccc|ccc} 1 \amp 2 \amp -1 \amp 1 \amp 0 \amp 0\\ 0 \amp 1 \amp 1 \amp 0 \amp 1 \amp 0\\ 0 \amp 0 \amp -2 \amp 1 \amp -2 \amp 1 \end{array}\right)\\ \amp \xrightarrow{R_3\to -R_3/2} \left(\begin{array}{ccc|ccc} 1 \amp 2 \amp -1 \amp 1 \amp 0 \amp 0\\ 0 \amp 1 \amp 1 \amp 0 \amp 1 \amp 0\\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 1 \amp -1/2 \amp 1 \amp -1/2 \end{array}\right)\\ \amp \xrightarrow{R_2\to R_2-R_3} \left(\begin{array}{ccc|ccc} 1 \amp 2 \amp -1 \amp 1 \amp 0 \amp 0\\ 0 \amp 1 \amp 0 \amp 1/2 \amp 0 \amp 1/2\\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 1 \amp -1/2 \amp 1 \amp -1/2 \end{array}\right)\\ \amp \xrightarrow{R_1\to R_1+R_3} \left(\begin{array}{ccc|ccc} 1 \amp 2 \amp 0 \amp 1/2 \amp 1 \amp -1/2\\ 0 \amp 1 \amp 0 \amp 1/2 \amp 0 \amp 1/2\\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 1 \amp -1/2 \amp 1 \amp -1/2 \end{array}\right)\\ \amp \xrightarrow{R_1\to R_1-2R_2} \left(\begin{array}{ccc|ccc} 1 \amp 0 \amp 0 \amp -1/2 \amp 1 \amp -3/2\\ 0 \amp 1 \amp 0 \amp 1/2 \amp 0 \amp 1/2\\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 1 \amp -1/2 \amp 1 \amp -1/2 \end{array}\right) \end{align*}
    So, we find that
    \begin{equation*} P^{-1}=\begin{pmatrix} -1/2 \amp 1 \amp -3/2 \\ 1/2 \amp 0 \amp 1/2 \\ -1/2 \amp 1 \amp -1/2\end{pmatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Therefore,
    \begin{equation*} [\bw]_B=P^{-1}\colvec{2 \\ 2 \\ -3} = \colvec{11/2 \\ -1/2 \\ 5/2}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    Changing basis is especially useful to understand the action of linear maps on vector spaces. We will focus on the case of linear maps from \(\R^n\) to \(\R^n\text{.}\) (I.e. the case of square matrices.)
    🔗

    Example 2.9.3.

    Let’s go back to the case where \(A=\begin{pmatrix} 1/2 \amp 1/2 \\ 1/2 \amp 1/2\end{pmatrix}\text{,}\) which describes the projection of \(\R^2\) onto the line \(y=x\text{.}\) We have seen that \(A\) has two eigenvalues: \(1\) and \(0\text{.}\) The eigenspace associated to \(1\) is given by \(y=x\text{,}\) while the eigenspace associated to \(0\) is \(y=-x\text{.}\) A basis for the \(1\)-eigenspace is \(\colvec{1 \\ 1}\text{,}\) while a basis for the \(0\)-eigenspace is \(\colvec{1 \\ -1}\text{.}\) Since they correspond to distinct eigenvalues, the vectors \(\bv_1=\colvec{1\\1}, \bv_2=\colvec{1 \\ -1}\) form a basis \(B\) of \(\R^2\text{.}\) This means that \(\phi_A(\bv_1)=\bv_1\) and \(\phi_A(\bv_2)=\bz\text{.}\) We will try to rewrite everything in terms of this new basis \(B\text{.}\)
    🔗
    The transition matrix is \(P=\begin{pmatrix} 1 \amp 1 \\ 1 \amp -1 \end{pmatrix}\text{.}\) In the basis \(B\text{,}\) we have
    \begin{equation*} [\bv_1]_B=P^{-1}\colvec{1 \\ 1}=\colvec{1 \\ 0}\quad\text{and}\quad [\bv_2]_B=P^{-1}\colvec{1 \\ -1}=\colvec{0 \\ 1}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Consider the matrix \([A]_B=\begin{pmatrix} 1 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 0\end{pmatrix}\text{.}\) Note that
    \begin{equation*} [A]_B[\bv_1]_B=\colvec{1 \\ 0}=[A\bv_1]_B\quad\text{and}\quad[A]_B[\bv_2]_B=\colvec{0 \\ 0}=[A\bv_2]_B\text{.} \end{equation*}
    In other words, the matrix \([A]_B\) takes vectors \(\bw\) written in the basis \(B\) and spits out \(A\bw\) written in the basis \(B\text{.}\) The matrix \([A]_B\) is the matrix corresponding to the linear map \(\varphi_A\) with respect to the basis \(B\text{.}\) I hope that we can agree that it is much simpler than the original matrix. In particular, the eigenvalues and eigenspaces are evident from this matrix.
    🔗
    🔗
    Let’s describe the process of writing the matrix of a linear map with respect to a given matrix in a slightly more general form. Let’s say that we have a linear map \(f:\R^n\rightarrow \R^n\) whose corresponding matrix (with respect to the basis consisting of standard unit vectors) is \(A\text{.}\) Let \(B\) be a basis of \(\R^n\text{,}\) and let \(P\) be the transition matrix from the standard unit basis to the new basis \(B\text{.}\) We are looking of an \(n\times n\) matrix \([A]_B\) such that
    \begin{equation*} [A]_B[\bw]_B=[A\bw]_B \end{equation*}
    for every vector \(\bw\in\R^n\text{.}\) We already know that \([\bw]_B=P^{-1}\bw\) and \([A\bw]_B=P^{-1}A\bw\text{.}\) Therefore,
    \begin{equation*} [A]_BP^{-1}\bw=P^{-1}A\bw \end{equation*}
    for every vector \(\bw\in\R^n\text{.}\) Multiplying both sides by \(P\text{,}\) we get
    \begin{equation*} P[A]_BP^{-1}\bw=A\bw \end{equation*}
    for every vector \(\bw\in\R^n\text{,}\) which is only possible if \(P[A]_BP^{-1}=A\text{.}\) Solving for \([A]_B\text{,}\) we obtain
    \begin{equation*} [A]_B=P^{-1}AP\text{.} \end{equation*}
    The matrix \([A]_B=P^{-1}AP\) is called the matrix of the linear map \(f\) with respect to the basis \(B\).
    🔗

    Example 2.9.4.

    Let’s go back to the matrix \(A=\begin{pmatrix} 1/2 \amp 1/2 \\ 1/2 \amp 1/2\end{pmatrix}\text{.}\) In the previous example we found \([A]_B\) for the basis \(\colvec{1 \\ 1}, \colvec{1\\-1}\text{.}\) Let’s use the formula that we just learnt to find \([A]_B\) and compare.
    \begin{equation*} [A]_B=P^{-1}AP=\begin{pmatrix}1/2 \amp 1/2 \\ 1/2 \amp -1/2\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} 1/2 \amp 1/2 \\ 1/2 \amp 1/2\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} 1 \amp 1 \\ 1 \amp -1\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}1 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 0\end{pmatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.9.5.

    Of course, the basis we are changing to is not required to be a basis of eigenvectors of a given matrix. For example, let’s consider the linear map \(f\) whose matrix in the the standard unit basis is \(A=\begin{pmatrix}1 \amp 3 \\ -1 \amp 2\end{pmatrix}\text{.}\) Let \(B\) be the basis \(\bv_1=\colvec{1 \\ 1}, \bv_2=\colvec{-1 \\ 0}\) of \(\R^2\text{.}\) Let’s find the matrix of \(f\) in the basis \(B\text{.}\)
    🔗
    The transition matrix is \(P=\begin{pmatrix}1 \amp -1 \\ 1 \amp 0\end{pmatrix}\text{.}\) The inverse of this matrix is \(P^{-1}=\begin{pmatrix} 1\amp -1 \\ 1 \amp 0\end{pmatrix}\text{.}\) Therefore,
    \begin{equation*} [f]_B=P^{-1}AP=\begin{pmatrix} -1 \amp 2 \\ -2 \amp -1\end{pmatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    If a linear map \(f:\R^n\rightarrow \R^n\) has eigenvalues \(\lambda_1,\lambda_2,\ldots,\lambda_r\) and the sum of the dimensions of the respective eigenspaces is \(n\text{,}\) then we can find a basis of eigenvectors for \(\R^n\text{.}\) The matrix of \(f\) in this basis will then be a diagonal matrix.
    🔗

    Example 2.9.6.

    Consider the linear map \(f:\R^3\rightarrow\R^3\) whose matrix in the standard unit basis is \(A=\begin{pmatrix} 3 \amp 1 \amp 1 \\ 2 \amp 2 \amp -2 \\ -1 \amp 1 \amp 5\end{pmatrix}\text{.}\) Its characteristic polynomial is
    \begin{equation*} p_A(x)=x^3-10x^2+32x-32=(x-4)^2(x-2)\text{.} \end{equation*}
    So, \(f\) has two eigenvalues: \(2\) and \(4\text{.}\) Let’s find the respective eigenspaces.
    🔗
    The \(2\)-eigenspace is the kernel of
    \begin{equation*} 2\Id_3-A=\begin{pmatrix}-1 \amp -1 \amp -1 \\ -2 \amp 0 \amp 2 \\ 1 \amp -1 \amp -3\end{pmatrix}\text{,} \end{equation*}
    which is the linear subspace of \(\R^3\) generated by \(\colvec{1 \\ -2 \\ 1}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    The \(4\)-eigenspace is the kernel of
    \begin{equation*} 4\Id_3-A=\begin{pmatrix} 1 \amp -1 \amp -1 \\ -2 \amp 2 \amp 2 \\ 1 \amp -1 \amp -1\end{pmatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    This is the \(2\)-dimensional subspace of \(\R^3\) spanned by the vectors \(\colvec{1 \\ 1 \\ 0}\) and \(\colvec{1 \\ 0 \\ 1}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Let’s find the matrix of \(f\) with respect to the basis \(B\) consisting of \(\bv_1=\colvec{1 \\ -2 \\ 1}, \bv_2=\colvec{1 \\ 1 \\ 0}, \bv_3=\colvec{1 \\ 0 \\ 1}\text{.}\) The transition matrix is
    \begin{equation*} P=\begin{pmatrix} 1 \amp 1 \amp 1 \\ -2 \amp 1 \amp 0 \\ 1 \amp 0 \amp 1 \end{pmatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Its inverse is
    \begin{equation*} P^{-1}=\begin{pmatrix} 1/2 \amp -1/2 \amp -1/2\\ 1 \amp 0 \amp -1 \\ -1/2 \amp 1/2 \amp 3/2 \end{pmatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    A quick calculation yields
    \begin{equation*} [f]_B=P^{-1}AP=\begin{pmatrix} 2 \amp 0 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 4 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 4\end{pmatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    An \(n\times n\) matrix for which there exists an invertible matrix \(P\) such that \(P^{-1}AP\) is diagonal is called diagonalizable. Not every matrix is diagonalizable.
    🔗

    Proposition 2.9.7.

    An \(n\times n\) matrix \(A\) is diagonalizable if and only if there exists a basis of \(\R^n\) consisting of eigenvectors of \(A\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Let’s start by assuming that \(A\) is a diagonalizable \(n\times n\) matrix. By definition, there exists an invertible \(n\times n\) matrix \(P\) such that \(B=P^{-1}AP\) is a diagonal matrix \(\diag(\lambda_1,\lambda_2,\ldots,\lambda_n)\text{.}\) (The \(\lambda_i\) may not be distinct.) Let \(\bv_1,\bv_2,\ldots,\bv_n\) be the vectors in \(\R^n\) whose coordinates in the standard unit basis are given by the columns of \(P\text{.}\) These form a basis of \(\R^n\) because the matrix \(P\) is invertible. Since \(B\be_i=\lambda_i\be_i\) for every \(i\text{,}\) it follows that \(A\bv_i=\lambda_i\bv_i\text{.}\) Therefore, \(\bv_1,\bv_2,\ldots,\bv_n\) is a basis of \(\R^n\) consisting of eigenvectors of \(A\text{.}\)
    🔗
    To prove the converse, let’s start by assuming that \(\bv_1,\bv_2,\ldots,\bv_n\) is a basis of \(\R^n\) consisting of eigenvectors of \(A\) with eigenvalues \(\lambda_1,\lambda_2,\ldots,\lambda_n\text{,}\) respectively. Let’s take the matrix \(P\) to be the one whose columns are the vectors \(\bv_1,\bv_2,\ldots,\bv_n\) (written with respect to the standard unit vectors). Then the matrix \(B=P^{-1}AP\) is the diagonal matrix \(\diag(\lambda_1,\lambda_2,\ldots,\lambda_n)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.9.8.

    Consider the matrix \(A=\begin{pmatrix} 1 \amp 1 \\ 0 \amp 1\end{pmatrix}\text{.}\) The only eigenvalue of this matrix is \(1\text{,}\) and the \(1\)-eigenspace is \(1\)-dimensional, which means that there is no basis of \(\R^2\) consisting of eigenvectors of \(A\text{.}\)
    🔗
