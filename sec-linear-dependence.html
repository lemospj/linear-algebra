    Section 2.3 Linear dependence

    Definition 2.3.1.

    Let \(\bv_1,\bv_2,\ldots,\bv_m\) be vectors in \(\R^n\text{.}\) A linear combination of \(\bv_1,\bv_2,\ldots,\bv_m\) is an expression of the form
    \begin{equation*} \lambda_1\bv_1+\lambda_2\bv_2+\ldots+\lambda_m\bv_m\text{,} \end{equation*}
    where \(\lambda_1,\lambda_2,\ldots,\lambda_m\) are real numbers. The scalars \(\lambda_1,\lambda_2,\ldots,\lambda_m\) are called the coefficients of the linear combination.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    The linear combination is the expression itself. Two different linear combinations can give rise to the same vector.
    ðŸ”—

    Example 2.3.2.

    The expression
    \begin{equation*} 2\colvec{1 \\ -1} + 3\colvec{2 \\ 3} - \colvec{1 \\ 0} \end{equation*}
    is a linear combination of the vectors \(\colvec{1 \\ -1}\text{,}\) \(\colvec{2 \\ 3}\) and \(\colvec{-1 \\ 0}\) in \(\R^2\text{.}\) The resulting vector is
    \begin{equation*} 2\colvec{1 \\ -1} + 3\colvec{2 \\ 3}-\colvec{1 \\ 0}=\colvec{2 \\ -2} + \colvec{6 \\ 9}-\colvec{1 \\ 0}=\colvec{7 \\ 7}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Here is a different linear combination of the same vectors that also gives rise to \(\colvec{7 \\ 7}\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} -\colvec{1 \\ -1} + 2\colvec{2 \\ 3} + 4\colvec{1 \\ 0} = \colvec{-1 \\ 1} + \colvec{4 \\ 6} + \colvec{4 \\ 0}=\colvec{7 \\ 7}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Example 2.3.3.

    In ExampleÂ 2.3.2 we saw that it is possible to express \(\colvec{7 \\ 7}\) in more than one way as linear combination of the vectors \(\colvec{1 \\ -1}, \colvec{2 \\ 3}\) and \(\colvec{1 \\ 0}\text{.}\) However, there is only one linear combination of the vectors \(\be_1=\colvec{1 \\ 0}\) and \(\be_2=\colvec{0 \\ 1}\) that results in \(\colvec{7 \\ 7}\text{.}\) Indeed, the problem is that of finding real numbers \(\lambda_1\) and \(\lambda_2\) such that
    \begin{equation*} \lambda_1\be_1+\lambda_2\be_2=\colvec{7 \\ 7}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    However,
    \begin{equation*} \lambda_1\be_1+\lambda_2\be_2= \lambda_1\colvec{1 \\ 0}+\lambda_2\colvec{0 \\ 1}= \colvec{\lambda_1 \\ \lambda_2}\text{,} \end{equation*}
    which means that we must have \(\lambda_1=\lambda_2=7\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Example 2.3.4.

    Sometimes it is not possible to represent a vector as a linear combination of prescribed vectors. For example, the vector
    \begin{equation*} \bv=\colvec{3 \\ 2 \\ 1} \end{equation*}
    in \(\R^3\) cannot be represented as a linear combination of the vectors
    \begin{equation*} \bw=\colvec{2 \\ 1 \\ 0}\quad \text{and}\quad \bu=\colvec{1 \\ 5 \\ 0} \end{equation*}
    because any linear combination of the vectors \(\bw\) and \(\bu\) will have \(0\) as its third coordinate.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    ExampleÂ 2.3.3 shows that sometimes there is at most one way to represent a vector as a linear combination of prescribed vectors. This phenomenon occurs when there are no linear relationships among the vectors that are to be combined.
    ðŸ”—

    Definition 2.3.5.

    We say that the vectors \(\bv_1,\bv_2,\ldots,\bv_m\in\R^n\) are said to be linearly dependent if there exist real numbers \(\lambda_1,\lambda_2,\ldots,\lambda_m\text{,}\) not all zero, such that
    \begin{equation*} \lambda_1\bv_1+\lambda_2\bv_2+\ldots+\lambda_m\bv_m=\bz\text{.} \end{equation*}
    If the vectors \(\bv_1,\bv_2,\ldots,\bv_m\) are not linearly dependent, then we say that they are linearly independent.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    The condition that the coefficients of the linear combination above are not all zero in the definition of linear dependence is crucial. Indeed, for any given set of vectors \(\bv_1,\bv_2,\ldots,\bv_m\text{,}\) it is always possible to find a trivial linear combination that represents the zero vector. All we need to do is to choose every coefficient to be \(0\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} 0\cdot\bv_1+0\cdot\bv_2+\ldots+0\cdot\bv_m=\bz\text{.} \end{equation*}
    The vectors \(\bv_1,\bv_2,\ldots,\bv_m\) are only linearly dependent if there exists a linear combination of these vectors representing the zero vector other than the trivial one. In particular, if you wish to prove that a given set of vectors \(\bv_1,\bv_2,\ldots,\bv_n\) is linearly independent, all you need to do is to show that if \(\lambda_1,\lambda_2,\ldots,\lambda_n\) are real numbers such that
    \begin{equation*} \lambda_1\bv_1+\lambda_2\bv_2+\ldots+\lambda_n\bv_n=\bz, \end{equation*}
    then all the \(\lambda_i\) equal \(0\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—

    Example 2.3.6.

    If \(\bv\in\R^n\) is non-zero, then the set consisting only of \(\bv\) is linearly independent. Indeed, if \(\lambda\bv=0\text{,}\) then we must have \(\lambda=0\) because, by assumption, \(\bv\neq \bz\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Example 2.3.7.

    The vectors \(\colvec{1 \\ 2}\) and \(\colvec{2 \\ 4}\) are linearly dependent. Indeed,
    \begin{equation*} 2\colvec{1 \\ 2}-\colvec{2 \\ 4}=\bz\text{.} \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Example 2.3.9.

    The set formed by the vectors \(\be_1:=\colvec{1 \\ 0}\) and \(\be_2:=\colvec{0 \\ 1}\) is linearly independent. Indeed, say that \(\lambda_1,\lambda_2\in\R\) are real numbers satisfying
    \begin{equation*} \lambda_1\be_1+\lambda_2\be_2=\bz\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Letâ€™s start by evaluating the left-hand side of the equation above:
    \begin{equation*} \lambda_1\be_1+\lambda_2\be_2=\lambda_1\colvec{1 \\ 0}+\lambda_2\colvec{0 \\ 1}=\colvec{\lambda_1 \\ \lambda_2}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    So we have
    \begin{equation*} \colvec{\lambda_1 \\ \lambda_2}= \colvec{0 \\ 0}\text{,} \end{equation*}
    which means that \(\lambda_1=0\) and \(\lambda_2=0\text{.}\) This shows that \(\be_1\) and \(\be_2\) are linearly independent.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Example 2.3.11.

    The vectors \(\colvec{1 \\ 2 \\ 3}\) and \(\colvec{2 \\ 1 \\ 3}\) are linearly independent. To see this, let \(\lambda\) and \(\mu\) be real scalars such that
    \begin{equation*} \lambda\colvec{1 \\ 2 \\ 3} + \mu\colvec{2 \\ 1 \\ 3} = \colvec{0 \\ 0 \\ 0}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Then
    \begin{equation*} \colvec{\lambda + 2\mu \\ 2\lambda + \mu \\ 3\lambda + 3\mu} = \colvec{0 \\ 0 \\ 0}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    In other words, we need to solve the system
    \begin{equation*} \left\{ \begin{alignedat}{5} \lambda \ampp + \ampp 2\mu \amp = \ampp 0 \\ 2\lambda \ampp + \ampp \mu \amp = \ampp 0 \\ 3\lambda \ampp + \ampp 3\mu \amp = \ampp 0 \end{alignedat} \right. \end{equation*}
    in the variables \(\lambda\) and \(\mu\text{.}\) But it is easy to see that the only solution to this system is given by \(\lambda=0\) and \(\mu=0\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    Let me mention an important generalization of ExampleÂ 2.3.9. Recall that the standard unit vectors in \(\R^n\) are the vectors
    \begin{equation*} \be_1=\colvec{1 \\ 0 \\ 0 \\ \vdots \\0},\qquad\be_2=\colvec{0 \\ 1 \\ 0 \\ \vdots \\0},\qquad\cdots,\qquad\be_n=\colvec{0 \\ 0 \\ 0 \\ \vdots \\1}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—

    Lemma 2.3.12.

    The set of standard unit vectors in \(\R^n\) is linearly independent.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Proof.

    Let \(\lambda_1,\ldots,\lambda_n\in\R\) be real numbers. If we denote by \(e_{ij}\) the \(j\)th entry of \(\be_i\text{,}\) then the \(j\)th entry of \(\lambda_1\be_1+\ldots+\lambda_n\be_n\) is \(\lambda_1e_{1j}+\lambda_2e_{2j}+\ldots+\lambda_ne_{nj}\text{.}\) By the definition of standard unit vectors, we have \(e_{ij}=0\) whenever \(i\neq j\text{,}\) and \(e_{ii}=1\) for every \(i\text{.}\) Therefore, the \(j\)th entry of \(\lambda_1\be_1+\ldots+\lambda_n\be_n\) is
    \begin{equation*} \lambda_1e_{1j}+\lambda_2e_{2j}+\ldots+\lambda_ne_{nj}=\lambda_j\text{.} \end{equation*}
    If we suppose that the \(\lambda_i\) are such that
    \begin{equation*} \lambda_1\be_1 + \lambda_2\be_2 + \ldots + \lambda_n \be_n=\bz\text{,} \end{equation*}
    we conclude that \(\lambda_j=0\) for every \(1\leq j\leq n\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Lemma 2.3.13.

    Let \(\bw\) be a vector in \(\R^n\text{,}\) and let \(\bv_1,\ldots,\bv_m\in\R^n\) be a set of \(m\) linearly independent vectors in \(\R^n\text{.}\) Then there is at most one linear combination \(\lambda_1\bv_1+\ldots+\lambda_m\bv_m\) of these \(m\) linearly independent vectors such that
    \begin{equation*} \bw=\lambda_1\bv_1+\ldots+\lambda_m\bv_m\text{.} \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Proof.

    Note that there may well be no linear combination of the linearly independent vectors that represents \(\bw\text{.}\) The claim is that if there is one, that is the only one. To prove this, suppose that we have two linear combinations of \(\bv_1,\ldots,\bv_m\) representing \(\bw\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} \lambda_1\bv_1+\lambda_2\bv_2+\ldots+\lambda_m\bv_m=\bw \end{equation*}
    and
    \begin{equation*} \mu_1\bv_1+\mu_2\bv_2+\ldots+\mu_m\bv_m=\bw\text{,} \end{equation*}
    where \(\lambda_1,\lambda_2,\ldots,\lambda_m,\mu_1,\mu_2,\ldots,\mu_m\in\R\text{.}\) If we subtract the second linear combination from the first we end up with
    \begin{equation*} (\lambda_1-\mu_1)\bv_1+(\lambda_2-\mu_2)\bv_2+\ldots+(\lambda_m-\mu_m)\bv_m=\bw-\bw=\bz\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Since the set \(\bv_1,\ldots,\bv_m\) is linearly independent, we must have \(\lambda_i-\mu_i=0\) for all \(i\text{.}\) Put differently, \(\lambda_i=\mu_i\) for all \(i\text{.}\) This means that the two linear combinations are actually the same.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    To be absolutely clear, LemmaÂ 2.3.13 does not say that you will be capable of representing any vector in \(\R^n\) using linear combinations of a given linearly independent set of vectors. For example, the set consisting of the vector \(\be_1=\colvec{1 \\ 0}\in\R^2\) alone is linearly independent. Linear combinations involving only \(\be_1\) are of the form \(\lambda\be_1\text{,}\) where \(\lambda\in\R\text{.}\) This means that the only vectors in \(\R^2\) that can be represented as linear combinations of \(\be_1\) are of the form \(\colvec{\lambda \\ 0}\text{.}\) This leaves out vectors like \(\be_2\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    What LemmaÂ 2.3.13 does say is that if it is possible to represent a vector using a linear combination of linearly independent vectors, then there is only one way of doing so.
    ðŸ”—
    Linearly independent sets of vectors of \(\R^n\) that are capable of representing any other vector in \(\R^n\) are very special and very important in linear algebra. We will discuss these sets in a later section.
    ðŸ”—
    Before finishing this section, I will state one more useful result. As with every result in these lecture notes, you should attempt to convince yourself of the truthfulness of the statement before looking at the proof. Sometimes you may be able to prove the results yourself, and you will learn a great deal by doing so. But at the very least, work out a couple of examples. The next lemma, in particular, is relatively easy to prove, so you could regard it as an exercise.

    Lemma 2.3.14.

    Let \(m\geq 2\) be an integer. If \(\bv_1,\bv_2,\ldots,\bv_m\in\R^n\) is a linearly dependent set of vectors, then at least one of these vectors can be written as a linear combination of the other vectors in the set.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Proof.

    If one of the vectors is the zero vector, then the result is trivial. Indeed, the zero vector can always be expressed as the trivial linear combination (i.e. coefficients are all \(0\)) of any finite set of vectors. We are therefore reduced to the case where all the vectors are non-zero.
    ðŸ”—
    Suppose that all the vectors \(\bv_1,\bv_2,\ldots,\bv_m\) are non-zero. Since they are linearly dependent, there is a set of \(m\) scalars \(\lambda_1,\lambda_2,\ldots,\lambda_m\text{,}\) not all zero, such that
    \begin{equation} \lambda_1\bv_1+\lambda_2\bv_2+\ldots+\lambda_m\bv_m=\bz\text{.}\tag{2.3.1} \end{equation}
    We may assume, without loss of generality, that \(\lambda_1\neq 0\text{,}\) because if this is not the case we can just reorder the vectors in the set and the scalars. (In other words, the labels that we assigned to the vectors are completely arbitrary and irrelevant.) But then, Equation (2.3.1) is equivalent to
    \begin{equation*} \lambda_2\bv_2+\ldots+\lambda_m\bv_m=-\lambda_1\bv_1\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Since \(\lambda_1\neq 0\text{,}\) we may divide both sides by \(-\lambda_1\text{,}\) from where we get
    \begin{equation*} \bv_1=-\frac{\lambda_2}{\lambda_1}\bv_2-\ldots-\frac{\lambda_m}{\lambda_1}\bv_m\text{.} \end{equation*}
    In other words, we wrote \(\bv_1\) as a linear combination of the other vectors in the set.
