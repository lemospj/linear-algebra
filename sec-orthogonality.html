    Section 2.5 Orthogonality

    This section will have a more geometric flavor than the previous ones. We will speak about distances and angles and see how these relate to the dot product between two vectors. We will see that every linear subspace admits a basis formed by orthogonal vectors of norm \(1\text{.}\) Finally, we will discuss the first real-world application of this course: the least squares method.
    🔗
    Let’s start by recalling that the norm of a vector \(\bv=\colvec{a_1\\ a_2\\ \vdots \\ a_n}\in\R^n\) was defined to be
    \begin{equation*} \lVert\bv\rVert = \sqrt{a_1^2+a_2^2+\ldots+a_n^2}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    This is the vector’s length. We also saw that there is a relationship between dot product and norm, namely \(\lVert \bv\rVert^2=\bv\cdot\bv\text{,}\) which follows directly from the definition.
    🔗
    The first problem that we want to solve is that of finding the closest point in a linear subspace of \(\R^n\) to a given point. To solve this, we will need the following fact.
    🔗

    Lemma 2.5.1.

    If \(\bv,\bw\in\R^n\) are two vectors, we have
    \begin{equation*} \bv\cdot\bw=\frac{1}{2}(\lVert \bv+\bw\rVert^2 - \lVert \bv\rVert^2-\lVert\bw\rVert^2)\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Note that
    \begin{equation*} (\bv+\bw)\cdot(\bv+\bw)=\bv\cdot\bv + 2\bv\cdot\bw+\bw\cdot\bw=\lVert\bv\rVert^2+2\bv\cdot\bw+\lVert\bw\rVert^2\text{.} \end{equation*}
    The result follows from solving the equation above for \(\bv\cdot\bw\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    A direct consequence of this result is that two vectors \(\bv,\bw\in\R^n\) are orthogonal to each other precisely when \(\bv\cdot\bw=0\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Corollary 2.5.2.

    Two vectors \(\bv,\bw\in\R^n\) are orthogonal to each other if and only if \(\bv\cdot\bw=0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    If the two vectors are collinear, then either \(\bv=\lambda\bw\) or \(\bw=\lambda\bv\) for some scalar \(\lambda\in\R\text{.}\) In either case, it can be easily checked that \(\bv\cdot\bw=0\) only when one of the two vectors is \(\bz\text{.}\) (We consider the zero vector to be orthogonal to all the vectors in \(\R^n\text{.}\))
    🔗
    Suppose now that the two vectors are not collinear. They then form two sides of the triangle with vertices the origin and the two heads of the vectors. The other side has length \(\lVert\bv-\bw\rVert\text{.}\) If the two vectors are perpendicular, then it follows immediately from the Pythagorean theorem that \(\lVert\bv-\bw\rVert^2=\lVert\bv\rVert^2+\lVert\bw\rVert^2\text{.}\) Using the formula in Lemma 2.5.1, we conclude that \(\bv\cdot(-\bw)=0\text{,}\) which yields \(\bv\cdot\bw=0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    But the converse to the Pythagorean theorem also holds: if a triangle with sides fo lengths \(a,b,c\) is such that \(a^2+b^2=c^2\text{,}\) then the angle formed by the sides of lengths \(a\) and \(b\) is a right angle. Therefore, if \(\bv\cdot\bw=0\text{,}\) then \(\lVert\bv-\bw\rVert^2=\lVert\bv\rVert^2+\lVert\bw\rVert^2\text{,}\) and so \(\bv\) and \(\bw\) must be orthogonal.
    🔗
    🔗
    In fact, using the law of the cosines, we can quickly find a generalization of Corollary 2.5.2.
    🔗

    Proposition 2.5.3.

    Let \(\bv\) and \(\bw\) be two non-zero vectors in \(\R^n\text{,}\) and let \(\theta\) be the angle between them. Then
    \begin{equation*} \bv\cdot\bw=\lVert\bv\rVert\lVert\bw\rVert\cos\theta\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    We show the case when \(\bv\) and \(\bw\) are not collinear, and collinear case as an exercise.
    🔗
    Consider the triangle defined by determining that two of its sides are the vectors \(\bv\) and \(\bw\text{.}\) The third side has length \(\lVert\bv-\bw\rVert\text{.}\) By the law of the cosines, we have
    \begin{equation*} \lVert\bv-\bw\rVert^2=\lVert\bv\rVert^2+\lVert\bw\rVert^2-2\lVert\bv\rVert\lVert\bw\rVert\cos\theta\text{.} \end{equation*}
    But Lemma 2.5.1 applied to the vectors \(\bv\) and \(-\bw\) states that \(\lVert\bv\rVert^2+\lVert\bw\rVert^2=\lVert\bv-\bw\rVert^2+2\bv\cdot\bw\text{.}\) Therefore,
    \begin{equation*} \lVert\bv-\bw\rVert^2=\lVert\bv-\bw\rVert^2+2\bv\cdot\bw-2\lVert\bv\rVert\lVert\bw\rVert\cos\theta\text{,} \end{equation*}
    which immediately leads to \(\bv\cdot\bw=\lVert\bv\rVert\lVert\bw\rVert\cos\theta\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    Let’s have a look at a concrete instance of our problem.
    🔗

    Example 2.5.4.

    Consider the linear subspace of \(\R^2\) defined as the set of solutions of the equation \(x-2y=0\text{.}\) This is a line of slope \(1/2\) passing through the origin. Let’s also consider the point \((1,2)\text{,}\) which does not lie on this line. Our objective is to find the point in the line \(x-2y=0\) that lies closest to \((1,2)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    The distance between the point \((1,2)\) and the line \(x-2y=0\) is the length of the line segment that starts at the point \((1,2)\) and ends at the line \(x-2y=0\text{,}\) intersecting it at a right angle. This line segment is depicted in Figure 2.5.5 as a dashed black line.
    🔗
    Consider now the vector \(\bv=(1,2)\) and the vector \(\bu\) defined as the vector whose head lies in the point of intersection between the line \(x-2y=0\) and the black dashed line in Figure 2.5.5. This is called the projection of \(\bv\) onto the line \(x-2y=0\text{.}\) The head of this vector is precisely the point on the line that lies the closest to \((1,2)\text{.}\) Define \(\bu^{\perp}:=\bv-\bu\text{.}\) This last vector has the same length as the dashed black line of Figure 2.5.5 and is parallel to it.
    🔗
    In the figure above, \(\theta\) is the angle between \(\bu\) and \(\bv\text{.}\) Using a little trigonometry, we find that \(\lVert\bu\rVert=\lVert\bv\rVert\cos\theta\text{.}\) If \(\bw\) is any non-zero vector on the line \(x-2y=0\text{,}\) we have \(\bu=\lambda\bw\) for some \(\lambda\in\R\text{.}\) This means that
    \begin{equation*} |\lambda|\lVert\bw\rVert=\lVert\lambda\bw\rVert=\lVert\bu\rVert=\lVert\bv\rVert\cos\theta\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Therefore,
    \begin{equation*} |\lambda|=\frac{\lVert\bv\rVert}{\lVert\bw\rVert}\cos\theta\text{.} \end{equation*}
    If we denote the angle between \(\bv\) and \(\bw\) by \(\theta(\bv,\bw)\text{,}\) then \(\cos\theta=\cos\theta(\bv,\bw)\) precisely when \(\lambda\geq 0\text{.}\) Otherwise, \(\cos\theta=-\cos\theta(\bv,\bw)\text{.}\) It follows that
    \begin{equation*} \lambda=\frac{\lVert\bv\rVert}{\lVert\bw\rVert}\cos\theta(\bv,\bw)\text{.} \end{equation*}
    So we can express \(\bu\) as a scalar multiple of \(\bw\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} \bu=\frac{\lVert\bv\rVert}{\lVert\bw\rVert}\cos\theta(\bv,\bw)\bw\text{.} \end{equation*}
    The last thing we need to do is to somehow remove any reference to the angle \(\theta\text{.}\) This can be done using the equality
    \begin{equation*} \bv\cdot\bw=\lVert\bv\rVert\lVert\bw\rVert\cos\theta(\bv,\bw)\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Therefore,
    \begin{equation*} \bu=\frac{\bv\cdot\bw}{\lVert\bw\rVert^2}\bw \end{equation*}
    for any non-zero vector \(\bw\) on the line \(x-2y=0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Let’s choose, for example, \(\bw=(2,1)\text{.}\) Then \(\bv\cdot\bw=4\text{,}\) \(\lVert\bw\rVert^2=5\text{,}\) which means that the projection of \(\bv\) onto the line \(x-2y=0\) is the vector
    \begin{equation*} \bu=\frac{4}{5}\colvec{2\\1}=\colvec{\frac{8}{5}\\ \frac{4}{5}}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    It is worth writing down the main result in the previous example in full generality, i.e. that the projection of a vector \(\bv\) in \(\R^n\) onto the line formed by a non-zero vector \(\bw\) is given by the expression
    \begin{equation*} \pr_{\bw}(\bv)=\frac{\bv\cdot\bw}{\lVert\bw\rVert^2}\bw\text{.} \end{equation*}
    To show that this always holds, we need to precisely define what we mean by projection to a line.
    🔗

    Definition 2.5.7.

    Let \(\bv\) be a vector in \(\R^n\text{,}\) and let \(\bw\) be a non-zero vector in \(\R^n\text{.}\) The projection of \(\bv\) onto the line \(\ell\) generated by \(\bw\) is defined to be the only vector \(\bp\) in \(\ell\) such that \(\bv-\bp\) is orthogonal to \(\bw\text{.}\) The projection of \(\bv\) onto \(\ell\) is denoted by \(\pr_{\ell}(\bv)\) or \(\pr_{\bw}(\bv)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Checkpoint 2.5.8.

    Show that if a vector \(\bv\) in \(\R^n\) is orthogonal to a vector \(\bw\text{,}\) then it must be orthogonal to every scalar multiple of \(\bw\text{.}\) Therefore, if \(\bw\) is non-zero and \(\bv\) is orthogonal to \(\bw\text{,}\) then \(\bv\) is orthogonal to every vector in the line generated by \(\bw\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    The definition above uses the fact that there is exactly one vector \(\bp\) in the line \(\ell\) generated by \(\bw\) such that \(\bv-\bp\) is orthogonal to \(\bw\text{,}\) but we never proved this.
    🔗

    Proposition 2.5.9.

    Let \(\bv\) be a vector in \(\R^n\text{,}\) and let \(\bw\) be a non-zero vector of \(\R^n\text{.}\) There is a unique vector \(\bp\) in the line generated by \(\bw\) such that \(\bv-\bp\) is orthogonal to \(\bw\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    There is at least one vector \(\bp\) in the line \(\ell\) generated by \(\bw\) such that \((\bv-\bp)\cdot\bw=0\text{.}\) Indeed, if we define
    \begin{equation*} \bp=\frac{\bv\cdot\bw}{\lVert\bw\rVert^2}\bw\text{,} \end{equation*}
    we easily see that
    \begin{equation*} (\bv-\bp)\cdot\bw=\bv\cdot\bw-\bp\cdot\bw=\bv\cdot\bw-\frac{\bv\cdot\bw}{\lVert\bw\rVert^2}\bw\cdot\bw=\bv\cdot\bw-\bv\cdot\bw=0\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Now we need to show that there can be no other such vector in the line \(\ell\) generated by \(\bw\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Suppose that \(\bq\) is another vector in the line generated by \(\bw\) such that \((\bv-\bq)\cdot\bw=0\text{.}\) We then must have
    \begin{equation*} 0=(\bv-\bp)\cdot\bu-(\bv-\bq)\cdot\bu=(\bq-\bp)\cdot\bu \end{equation*}
    for every \(\lambda\in\R\text{.}\) Since both \(\bp\) and \(\bq\) are scalar multiples of \(\bw\) (this is what it means to lie in the line generated by \(\bw\)), the difference \(\bq-\bp\) is a scalar multiple of \(\bw\text{,}\) and so lies in the line generated by \(\bw\text{.}\) Therefore, a particular instance of the equation above is \((\bq-\bp)\cdot(\bq-\bp)=0\text{.}\) Bu this means that \(\lVert\bq-\bp\rVert^2=0\text{,}\) which is only possible if \(\bq-\bp=\bz\text{.}\) This means that \(\bq=\bp\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Corollary 2.5.10.

    Let \(\bw\) be a non-zero vector in \(\R^n\text{.}\) The projection of a vector \(\bv\) in \(\R^n\) onto the line generated by \(\bw\) is given by the expression
    \begin{equation*} \pr_{\bw}(\bv)=\frac{\bv\cdot\bw}{\lVert\bw\rVert^2}\bw\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    We saw during the proof of Proposition 2.5.9 that the vector \(\bp=\frac{\bv\cdot\bw}{\lVert\bw\rVert^2}\bw\) lies in the line generated by \(\bw\) and is such that \((\bv-\bp)\cdot\bw=0\text{.}\) By the uniqueness established by Proposition 2.5.9, we conclude that this must be the projection of \(\bv\) onto the line generated by \(\bw\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    You now know how to project vectors onto lines, and you will soon learn how to project vectors onto any linear subspace. But before we get into that, we need to learn one more thing, namely that every linear subspace admits an orthonormal basis.
    🔗

    Definition 2.5.11.

    A basis \(B\) of a linear subspace \(V\) or \(\R^n\) is said to be an orthonormal basis if the following two conditions hold:
    1. \(\lVert\bv\rVert=1\) for every vector \(\bv\) in \(B\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. \(\bv\cdot\bw=0\) for every pair of distinct vectors \(\bv,\bw\in B\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    In other words, an orthonormal basis is a basis consisting of unit vectors that are orthogonal to each other.
    🔗

    Example 2.5.12.

    The standard unit vectors in \(\R^n\) are an example of an orthonormal basis of \(\R^n\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.5.13.

    Consider the vectors \(\bv=\colvec{2 \\ 1 \\ 0}\) and \(\bw=\colvec{-1 \\ 0 \\1}\text{.}\) They are linearly independent, and so they span a \(2\)-dimensional linear subspace \(V\) of \(\R^3\text{.}\) They are form a basis for \(V\text{,}\) of course. However, they do not form an orthonormal basis. Indeed, \(\lVert\bv\rVert=\sqrt{5}\text{,}\) \(\lVert\bw\rVert=\sqrt{2}\) and \(\bv\cdot\bw=-2\text{.}\) However, we can use this basis to obtain an orthonormal basis of \(V\text{.}\)
    🔗
    The first step is to find an orthogonal basis, i.e. one that consists of pairwise orthogonal vectors. We will use \(\bv_1=\bv\) as our first basis vector. For the second one, we start by considering the projection of \(\bw\) onto the line generated by \(\bv\text{.}\) As we now know, this is given by the expression
    \begin{equation*} \pr_{\bv}(\bw)=\frac{\bw\cdot\bv}{\lVert\bv\rVert^2}\bv=-\frac{2}{5}\bv=\colvec{-\frac{4}{5} \\ -\frac{2}{5} \\ 0}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    The vector \(\bv_2=\bw-\pr_{\bv}(\bw)\) is orthogonal to \(\bv\text{.}\) Explicitly, it is given by
    \begin{equation*} \bv_2=\bw-\pr_{\bv}(\bw)=\colvec{-\frac{1}{5} \\ \frac{2}{5} \\ 1}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    The vectors \(\bv_1\) and \(\bv_2\) are now orthogonal vectors in \(V\text{,}\) and so they are also linearly independent. Since \(\dim(V)=2\text{,}\) the vectors \(\bv_1=\colvec{2 \\ 1 \\ 0}\) and \(\bv_2=\colvec{-\frac{1}{5} \\ \frac{2}{5} \\ 1}\) form an orthogonal basis of \(V\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Now all we need to do is normalize each vector, i.e. scale them so that they become unit vectors. Since \(\lVert\bv_1\rVert=\sqrt{5}\) and \(\bu_2 = \frac{\sqrt{30}}{5}\text{,}\) we conclude that
    \begin{equation*} \bu_1=\colvec{\frac{2}{\sqrt{5}} \\ \frac{1}{\sqrt{5}} \\ 0}\quad\text{ and }\quad \bu_2=\colvec{-\frac{1}{\sqrt{30}} \\ \frac{2}{\sqrt{30}} \\ \frac{5}{\sqrt{30}}} \end{equation*}
    is an orthonormal basis for \(V\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    The process that we used in the example above to find an orthonormal basis from a known basis of a vector space \(V\) is called the Gram-Schmidt orthogonalization process. In general, if \(V\) is a linear subspace of \(\R^n\) and we have a basis \(\bw_1,\ldots,\bw_m\) of it, the Gram-Schmidt orthogonalization process is an algorithm that takes these data and returns an orthonormal basis of \(V\text{.}\) The algorithm has two phases. The first phase creates an orthogonal basis. The second phase normalizes each vector in the orthogonal basis obtained in the first phase. Here’s how the process works.
    🔗
    The procedure to obtain an orthogonal basis is recursive. The objective is to use the starting basis \(\bw_1,\bw_2,\ldots,\bw_m\) to find an orthogonal basis \(\bv_1,\bv_2,\ldots,\bv_m\text{.}\) The first vector \(\bv_1\) is defined to be the first vector of the original basis, i.e. \(\bw_1\text{.}\) We now define the vector \(\bv_2\) as follows:
    \begin{equation*} \bv_2=\bw_2-\pr_{\bv_1}(\bw_2)\text{.} \end{equation*}
    We can easily see that \(\bv_1\) and \(\bv_2\) are orthogonal:
    \begin{equation*} \bv_1\cdot\bv_2=\bv_1\cdot(\bw_2-\pr_{\bv_1}(\bw_2))=\bv_1\cdot\bw_2-\frac{\bw_2\cdot\bv_1}{\lVert\bv_1\rVert^2}\bv_1\cdot\bv_1=0\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Now suppose that we already have \(k\) orthogonal vectors \(\bv_1,\bv_2,\ldots,\bv_k\) in \(V\text{.}\) We define the next one as
    \begin{equation*} \bv_{k+1} = \bw_{k+1} - \pr_{\bv_1}(\bw_{k+1})-\pr_{\bv_2}(\bw_{k+1})-\ldots-\pr_{\bv_k}(\bw_k)\text{.} \end{equation*}
    It is now easy to check that \(\bv_{k+1}\) is orthogonal to every vector among \(\bv_1,\bv_2,\ldots,\bv_k\text{.}\) Indeed, if \(1\leq i \leq k\text{,}\) we have
    \begin{equation*} \bv_{k+1}\cdot\bv_i=\bw_{k+1}\cdot\bv_i - \frac{\bw_{k+1}\cdot \bv_i}{\lVert\bv_i\rVert^2}\bv_i\cdot\bv_i=0\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Proceeding recursively, we eventually end up with an orthogonal set of vectors \(\bv_1,\bv_2,\ldots,\bv_m\text{,}\) which must necessarily form a basis of \(V\text{.}\)
    🔗
    To normalize these vectors, we now simply divide each by its norm. In other words, for each \(i\) we define \(\bu_i=\frac{1}{\lVert\bv_i\rVert}\bv_i\text{.}\) The set \(\bu_1,\bu_2,\ldots,\bu_m\) is now an orthonormal basis of \(V\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Theorem 2.5.14.

    Every linear subspace of \(\R^n\) admits an orthonormal basis.
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.5.15.

    Consider the vectors \(\bw_1=\colvec{1 \\ 2 \\ 0 \\ -1}\text{,}\) \(\bw_2=\colvec{0 \\ 2 \\ 1 \\ 0}\) and \(\bw_3=\colvec{1 \\ 1 \\ 1 \\ 1}\text{.}\) Let \(W\) denote its span, which is a linear subspace of \(\R^4\text{.}\) They are linearly independent (show this), and so \(\bw_1,\bw_2,\bw_3\) constitutes a basis of \(W\text{.}\) Let’s use the Gram-Schmidt orthogonalization process to find an orthonormal basis \(\bu_1,\bu_2,\bu_3\) of \(W\text{.}\)
    🔗
    First, we define \(\bv_1=\bw_1\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Now define
    \begin{equation*} \bv_2=\bw_2-\pr_{\bv_1}(\bw_2)=\bw_2-\frac{\bw_2\cdot\bv_1}{\lVert\bv_1\rVert^2}\bv_1=\bw_2-\frac{2}{3}\bv_1=\colvec{-\frac{2}{3} \\ \frac{2}{3} \\ 1 \\ \frac{2}{3}}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Finally, define
    \begin{equation*} \bv_3=\bw_3 - \pr_{\bv_1}(\bw_3)-\pr_{\bv_2}(\bw_3)=\colvec{\frac{8}{7}\\ -\frac{1}{7} \\ \frac{2}{7}\\ \frac{6}{7}}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    We now need to normalize the vectors \(\bv_1,\bv_2,\bv_3\text{.}\) Since \(\lVert\bv_1\rVert=\sqrt{6}\text{,}\) \(\lVert\bv_2\rVert=\frac{\sqrt{7}}{\sqrt{3}}\) and \(\lVert\bv_3\rVert=\frac{\sqrt{15}}{\sqrt{7}}\text{,}\) we conclude that an orthonormal basis for \(W\) is
    \begin{equation*} \bu_1=\colvec{\frac{1}{\sqrt{6}} \\ \frac{2}{\sqrt{6}} \\ 0 \\ -\frac{1}{\sqrt{6}}},\quad \bu_2=\colvec{-\frac{2\sqrt{3}}{3\sqrt{7}} \\ \frac{2\sqrt{3}}{3\sqrt{7}} \\ \frac{\sqrt{3}}{\sqrt{7}} \\ \frac{2\sqrt{3}}{3\sqrt{7}}}\quad\text{and}\quad\bu_3=\colvec{\frac{8\sqrt{7}}{7\sqrt{15}} \\ -\frac{\sqrt{7}}{7\sqrt{15}} \\ \frac{2\sqrt{7}}{7\sqrt{15}} \\ \frac{6\sqrt{7}}{7\sqrt{15}}}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
