Proposition 2.5.23.

Let \(V\) be a linear subspace of \(\R^n\) and let \(f:\R^n\rightarrow\R^m\) be a linear map. Then \(f(V)\) is a linear subspace of \(\R^m\text{.}\)
🔗
🔗

Proof.

If \(\bw_1\) and \(\bw_2\) are two vectors in \(f(V)\text{,}\) then, by definition, there are two vectors \(\bv_1\) and \(\bv_2\) in \(V\) such that \(f(\bv_1)=\bw_1\) and \(f(\bv_2)=\bw_2\text{.}\) Since \(V\) is a linear subspace of \(\R^n\text{,}\) it follows that \(\bv_1+\bv_2\in V\text{.}\) Therefore, \(\bw_1+\bw_2=f(\bv_1)+f(\bv_2)=f(\bv_1+\bv_2)\) is a vector in \(f(V)\text{.}\)
🔗
Now let \(\bw\) be a vector in \(f(V)\) and let \(\lambda\in\R\) be a scalar. Using the definition of image of a linear map again, we know that there exists a vector \(\bv\in V\) such that \(f(\bv)=\bw\text{.}\) Because \(V\) is a linear subspace of \(\R^n\text{,}\) we also know that \(\lambda\bv\in V\text{,}\) which implies that \(f(\lambda\bv)\) lies in \(f(V)\text{.}\) Using the linearity of \(f\text{,}\) we see that \(f(\lambda\bv)=\lambda f(\bv)=\lambda\bw\text{.}\) Therefore, \(\lambda\bw\in f(V)\text{.}\)
