Example 2.8.7.

Consider the matrix \(A=\begin{pmatrix} 1 \amp 2 \\ -1 \amp 3\end{pmatrix}\text{.}\) It is easy to check that the rank of this matrix is \(2\text{.}\) Since this is a \(2\times 2\) matrix, this means that the matrix is invertible. Let’s try to find its inverse. Since \(A\colvec{1 \\ 0}=\colvec{1 \\ -1}\) and \(A\colvec{0 \\ 1}=\colvec{2 \\ 3}\text{,}\) the inverse matrix should undo this, i.e.
\begin{equation*} A^{-1}\colvec{1 \\ -1} = \colvec{1 \\ 0}\quad\text{and}\quad A^{-1}\colvec{2 \\ 3} = \colvec{0 \\ 1}\text{.} \end{equation*}
Our job is to find the pre-images of \(\colvec{1 \\ 0}\) and \(\colvec{0 \\ 1}\) under the matrix \(A\text{.}\) One nice way of doing this is by setting up the following matrix:
\begin{equation*} \left( \begin{array}{cc|cc} 1 \amp 2 \amp 1 \amp 0 \\ -1 \amp 3 \amp 0 \amp 1 \end{array} \right) \end{equation*}
the left side of which is \(A\) and whose right side is the identity matrix. We now perform Gaussian elimination until the matrix on the left-hand side is the identity matrix. Once we achieve that point, the matrix on the righ-hand side will be our inverse.
\begin{align*} \left( \begin{array}{cc|cc} 1 \amp 2 \amp 1 \amp 0 \\ -1 \amp 3 \amp 0 \amp 1 \end{array} \right)\amp \xrightarrow{R_2\to R_2+R_1} \left( \begin{array}{cc|cc} 1 \amp 2 \amp 1 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 5 \amp 1 \amp 1 \end{array} \right)\\ \amp \xrightarrow{R_2\to R_2/5} \left( \begin{array}{cc|cc} 1 \amp 2 \amp 1 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 1 \amp 1/5 \amp 1/5 \end{array} \right)\\ \amp \xrightarrow{R_1\to R_1-2R_2} \left( \begin{array}{cc|cc} 1 \amp 0 \amp 3/5 \amp -2/5 \\ 0 \amp 1 \amp 1/5 \amp 1/5 \end{array} \right) \end{align*}
The inverse of \(A\) is the matrix
\begin{equation*} A^{-1} = \begin{pmatrix} \frac{3}{5} \amp -\frac{2}{5} \\ \frac{1}{5} \amp \frac{1}{5}\end{pmatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}
It can be readily checked that \(AA^{-1}=A^{-1}A=\Id_2\text{.}\)
