Proposition 2.8.6.

A square matrix \(A\) is invertible if and only if its columns form a linearly independent set of vectors.
🔗
🔗

Proof.

If the columns of \(A\) are linearly independent, then it follows immediately that they span the whole of \(\R^n\text{,}\) which means that the matrix is invertible.
🔗
Assume now that \(A\) is invertible. We want to show that the columns of \(A\) form a linearly independent set of vectors. Being invertible means that the kernel consists of the zero vector. Therefore, the only solution to
\begin{equation*} A\colvec{x_1 \\ x_2 \\ \vdots \\ x_n}=x_1\colvec{a_{11}\\a_{21}\\ \vdots \\ a_{n1}} + x_2\colvec{a_{12} \\ a_{22} \\ \vdots \\ a_{n2}} + \ldots + x_n\colvec{a_{1n} \\ a_{2n} \\ \vdots \\ a_{nn}}=\bz \end{equation*}
is \(x_1=x_2=\ldots=x_n=0\text{.}\) but this means precisely that the columns of \(A\) form a linearly independent set of vectors.
