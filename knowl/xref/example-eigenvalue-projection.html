Example 2.7.2.

In the example above where \(A=\begin{pmatrix}\frac{1}{2} \amp \frac{1}{2} \\ \frac{1}{2} \amp \frac{1}{2} \end{pmatrix}\text{,}\) the vector \(\colvec{1 \\ 1}\) is an eigenvector associated to the eigenvalue \(1\text{,}\) while the vector \(\colvec{1 \\ -1}\) is an eigenvector associated to the eigenvalue \(0\text{.}\) So \(1\) and \(0\) are eigenvalues of \(A\text{.}\) They turn out to be all the eigenvalues of \(A\text{.}\) (We will soon see how this can be shown.) What other eigenvectors are there associated to \(0\text{?}\) To answer this question we must solve the system
\begin{equation*} A\colvec{x \\ y} = \colvec{0 \\ 0}\text{.} \end{equation*}
It can be easily see that the solutions to this system are all the vectors of the form \(\colvec{x \\ -x}\text{.}\) This means that the eigenvectors associated to \(0\) are precisely the non-zero vectors of the form \(\colvec{x \\ -x}\text{.}\) This is precisely the orthogonal space to the line \(y=x\text{.}\)
🔗
Let’s now try to find all the vectors associated to the eigenvalue \(1\text{.}\) This boils down to solving the system
\begin{equation*} A\colvec{x \\ y}=\colvec{x \\ y}\text{,} \end{equation*}
or, equivalently, the system
\begin{equation*} (A-\Id_2)\colvec{x \\ y}=\colvec{0 \\ 0}\text{.} \end{equation*}
It can be easily see that all the solutions to this system are the vectors of the form \(\colvec{x \\ x}\text{,}\) i.e. all the vectors in the line \(y=x\text{.}\) The eigenvectors associated to the eigenvalue \(1\) are then the non-zero vectors in the line \(y=x\text{.}\)
