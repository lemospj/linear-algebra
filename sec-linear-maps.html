    Section 2.8 More on linear maps

    In this section we will take a deeper dive into linear maps. We will prove one of the most important results in linear algebra: the rank-nullity theorem. We will also discuss invertible matrices and learn what the determinant of a square matrix can tell us about the number of solutions to a linear system.
    🔗
    We already studied two important linear subspaces associated to a linear map \(f:\R^n\rightarrow \R^m\text{:}\) its kernel, which is a linear subspace of the domain, and its image, which is a linear subspace of the codomain. We know how to find bases for those and, therefore, how to calculate their dimensions. It turns out that the dimensions of these two linear subspaces are intimately connected.
    🔗

    Theorem 2.8.1. Rank-Nullity Theorem.

    Let \(f:\R^n\rightarrow \R^m\) be a linear map. Then
    \begin{equation*} \dim(\ker(f))+\dim(\Img(f))=n\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Say that \(r\) and is the dimension of the kernel of \(f\) and \(s\) is the dimension of the image of \(f\text{.}\) Choose a basis \(\bv_1,\ldots,\bv_r\) for the kernel of \(f\text{.}\) We know that we can complete it to a basis \(\bv_1,\ldots,\bv_r,\bv_{r+1},\ldots,\bv_n\) of \(\R^n\text{.}\) Since this is a basis of \(\R^n\text{,}\) the image of \(f\) is spanned by the vectors
    \begin{equation*} f(\bv_1),\ldots,f(\bv_r),f_(bv_{r+1}),\ldots,f(\bv_n)\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Since \(\bv_1,\ldots,\bv_r\) are in the kernel of \(f\text{,}\) we conclude that \(f(\bv_i)=\bz\) if \(0\leq i\leq r\text{.}\) It follows that \(\Img(f)\) is spanned by \(f(\bv_{r+1}),\ldots,f(\bv_n)\text{.}\) We will now show that the vectors \(f(\bv_{r+1}),\ldots,f(\bv_n)\) form a linearly independent set, and so are a basis of \(\Img(f)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Let \(\lambda_{r+1},\ldots,\lambda_n\in\R\) be scalars such that
    \begin{equation*} \lambda_{r+1}f(\bv_{r+1})+\ldots+\lambda_nf(\bv_n)=\bz\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Using the linearity of \(f\) we see that this is equivalent to saying that
    \begin{equation*} f(\lambda_{r+1}\bv_{r+1}+\ldots+\lambda_n\bv_n)=\bz\text{,} \end{equation*}
    or, in other words, that \(\lambda_{r+1}\bv_{r+1}+\ldots+\lambda_n\bv_n\) is an element of the kernel of \(f\text{.}\) Since the kernel is spanned by \(\bv_1,\ldots,\bv_r\text{,}\) this means that there are coefficients \(\mu_1,\ldots,\mu_r\) such that
    \begin{equation*} \mu_1\bv_1+\ldots+\mu_r\bv_r=\lambda_{r+1}\bv_{r+1}+\ldots+\lambda_n\bv_n\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Put differently but equivalently,
    \begin{equation*} \mu_1\bv_1+\ldots+\mu_r\bv_r-\lambda_{r+1}\bv_{r+1}-\ldots-\lambda_n\bv_n=\bz\text{.} \end{equation*}
    However, the vectors \(\bv_1,\ldots,\bv_r,\bv_{r+1},\ldots,\bv_n\) are linearly independent because they form a basis of \(\R^n\text{.}\) This means that
    \begin{equation*} \mu_1=\ldots=\mu_r=\lambda_{r+1}=\ldots=\lambda_n=0\text{.} \end{equation*}
    In particular, we conclude that \(f(\bv_{r+1}),\ldots,f(\bv_n)\) is a set of linearly independent vectors in \(\R^m\text{.}\) Since they span \(\Img(f)\text{,}\) they are a basis for \(\Img(f)\text{.}\) Therefore, \(\dim(\Img(f))=n-r=n-\dim(\ker(f))\text{,}\) as we wanted to show.
    🔗
    🔗

    Remark 2.8.2.

    The dimension of the kernel of a linear map is called the nullity of the linear map, while the dimension of the image is called the rank of the linear map, hence the name of the theorem.
    🔗
    🔗
    Sometimes the rank of a linear map is called the column rank. This is because if \(A\) is the \(m\times n\) matrix corresponding to the linear map in question, then the rank is precisely the dimension of the linear subspace of \(\R^m\) spanned by the columns of \(A\text{.}\) The row rank of \(A\) refers to the linear subspace of \(\R^n\) spanned by the rows of \(A\text{.}\) These are two different linear subspaces (one lives in the domain and the other in the codomain), but they turn out to have the same dimension, i.e. the row rank equals the column rank.
    🔗

    Corollary 2.8.3.

    Let \(A\) be an \(m\times n\) matrix. Then the row rank of \(A\) equals its column rank.
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    This is a consequence of the rank-nullity theorem and of a fact that we discussed before. First, by the rank-nullity theorem, we know that
    \begin{equation*} \dim(\Img(A))=n-\dim(\ker(A))\text{.} \end{equation*}
    In other words, the column rank of \(A\) equals \(n-dim(\ker(A))\text{.}\) We have seen that \(\ker(A)=\Img(A^T)^{\perp}\text{.}\) Since \(\R^n=\Img(A^T)\oplus\Img(A^T)^{\perp}\text{,}\) we conclude that
    \begin{equation*} \dim(\Img(A^T))=n-\dim(\Img(A^T)^{\perp})=n-\dim(\ker(A))\text{.} \end{equation*}
    But \(\dim(\Img(A^T))\) is precisely the row rank of \(A\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    One important consequence of this result is that we can choose to use column of row operations to calculate the dimension of the image of a linear map.
    🔗

    Example 2.8.4.

    Consider the linear map given by the matrix \(\begin{pmatrix} 1 \amp -6 \amp 8 \\ 1 \amp -43 \amp 3\end{pmatrix}\text{.}\) Let’s calculate the rank of \(A\text{.}\) Since the row rank equals the column rank, we may as well calculate the row rank. In order to do that, we use elementary row operations until the row rank is clear.
    \begin{equation*} \begin{pmatrix} 1 \amp -6 \amp 8 \\ 1 \amp -43 \amp 3\end{pmatrix}\xrightarrow{R_2\to R_2-R_1}\begin{pmatrix} 1 \amp -6 \amp 8 \\ 0 \amp -37 \amp -5\end{pmatrix}\text{,} \end{equation*}
    and it is clear from the last matrix that the row rank is \(2\text{.}\) Therefore, the rank of \(A\) is \(2\) as well.
    🔗
    Note that the matrix \(A\) corresponds to a linear map with domain \(\R^3\text{.}\) Using the rank-nullity theorem, we can also conclude that the dimension of the kernel is \(3-2=1\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    Let’s now look at a linear map \(f:\R^n\rightarrow \R^n\text{,}\) i.e. one whose domain and codomain match. If the kernel of \(f\) happens to be \(0\)-dimensional (which means that it consists of the zero vector alone), then the image of \(f\) must be \(n\)-dimensional. But the only \(n\)-dimensional linear subspace of \(\R^n\) is \(\R^n\) itself. What this means is that the image of \(f\) is \(\R^n\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Definition 2.8.5.

    A linear map \(f:\R^n\rightarrow \R^n\) for which there exists a linear map \(g:\R^n\rightarrow \R^n\) such that \(f\circ g=g\circ f=\Id_n\) is called invertible. The linear map \(g\) is called the inverse of \(f\) and is denoted by \(f^{-1}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    This definition can be made in terms of square matrices, of course. An \(n\times n\) matrix \(A\) is said to be invertible if there exists an \(n\times n\) matrix \(B\) such that \(BA=AB=\Id_n\text{.}\) This matrix \(B\) is often denoted by \(A^{-1}\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Proposition 2.8.6.

    Let \(A\) be an \(n\times n\) matrix. The following statements are equivalent:
    1. \(A\) is invertible.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. \(\displaystyle \ker(A)=\{\bz\}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    3. \(\displaystyle \Img(A)=\R^n\)
      🔗
      🔗
    4. The columns of \(A\) are linearly independent.
      🔗
      🔗
    5. \(\displaystyle \det(A)\neq 0\)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    The fact that \((b)\) and \((c)\) are equivalent follows from the rank-nullity theorem. Indeed, we know that \(\dim(\ker(A))+\dim(\Img(A))=n\text{.}\) If \(\ker(A)=\{\bz\}\text{,}\) then \(\dim(\ker(A))=0\text{,}\) which means that \(\dim(\Img(A))=n\text{.}\) Since the codomain of the linear map associated to an \(n\by n\) amtrix is \(\R^n\text{,}\) this means that the image must be the whole of \(\R^n\text{.}\) Conversely, if \(\Img(A)=\R^n\text{,}\) then \(\dim(\Img(A))=n\text{,}\) which means that \(\dim(\ker(A))=0\text{,}\) and so \(\ker(A)=\{\bz\}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    If \(A\) is invertible, then it is easy to see that \(\ker(A)=\{\bz\}\text{.}\) If \(\bv\in\R^n\) is such that \(A\bv=\bz\text{,}\) then we may multiply both sides on the left by the inverse \(A^{-1}\) of \(A\) to get \(A^{-1}A\bv=A^{-1}\bz\text{.}\) Since \(A^{-1}A=\Id_n\text{,}\) we conclude that \(\bv=\bz\text{.}\)
    🔗
    To prove that statement (b) implies statement (a), one needs to observe that since (b) and (c) are equivalent, statement (b) implies that the linear map associated to \(A\) is bijective. We can then use Exercise 2.2.21 to show that the inverse of a bijective linear map is a linear map too. This shows that \(A\) is invertible (as a linear map).
    🔗
    Statements (c) and (d) are easily seen to be equivalent because \(\Img(A)\) is precisely the linear subspace of \(\R^n\) spanned by the columns of \(A\text{.}\) So, at this point, we know that statements (a), (b), (c) and (d) are equivalent to each other.
    🔗
    Let’s show that (a) implies (e). If \(A\) is invertible, then it has an inverse \(A^{-1}\text{,}\) and \(A^{-1}A=\Id_n\text{.}\) Therefore, \(\det(A^{-1})\det(A)=\det(\Id_n)=1\text{.}\) In particular, \(\det(A)\neq 0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Finally, all we need to do is to show that statement (e) implies one of the other statements. Exercise (add reference) is precisely the statement that (e) implies (d).
    🔗
    🔗
    If \(A\) is an invertible matrix, then the linear system \(A\bx=\bb\) always has a solution (because every vector \(\bb\) is in the image) and there is exactly one solution to the system (essentially because the kernel consists of the zero vector). Therefore, each vector in \(\R^n\) is the image of exactly one vector under the matrix \(A\text{.}\) We may then consider the inverse function.
    🔗
    One important exercise is to check that if \(A\) is an invertible matrix, then the inverse is unique.
    🔗

    Example 2.8.7.

    Consider the matrix \(A=\begin{pmatrix} 1 \amp 2 \\ -1 \amp 3\end{pmatrix}\text{.}\) It is easy to check that the rank of this matrix is \(2\text{.}\) Since this is a \(2\times 2\) matrix, this means that the matrix is invertible. Let’s try to find its inverse. Since \(A\colvec{1 \\ 0}=\colvec{1 \\ -1}\) and \(A\colvec{0 \\ 1}=\colvec{2 \\ 3}\text{,}\) the inverse matrix should undo this, i.e.
    \begin{equation*} A^{-1}\colvec{1 \\ -1} = \colvec{1 \\ 0}\quad\text{and}\quad A^{-1}\colvec{2 \\ 3} = \colvec{0 \\ 1}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Our job is to find the pre-images of \(\colvec{1 \\ 0}\) and \(\colvec{0 \\ 1}\) under the matrix \(A\text{.}\) One nice way of doing this is by setting up the following matrix:
    \begin{equation*} \left( \begin{array}{cc|cc} 1 \amp 2 \amp 1 \amp 0 \\ -1 \amp 3 \amp 0 \amp 1 \end{array} \right) \end{equation*}
    the left side of which is \(A\) and whose right side is the identity matrix. We now perform Gaussian elimination until the matrix on the left-hand side is the identity matrix. Once we achieve that point, the matrix on the righ-hand side will be our inverse.
    \begin{align*} \left( \begin{array}{cc|cc} 1 \amp 2 \amp 1 \amp 0 \\ -1 \amp 3 \amp 0 \amp 1 \end{array} \right)\amp \xrightarrow{R_2\to R_2+R_1} \left( \begin{array}{cc|cc} 1 \amp 2 \amp 1 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 5 \amp 1 \amp 1 \end{array} \right)\\ \amp \xrightarrow{R_2\to R_2/5} \left( \begin{array}{cc|cc} 1 \amp 2 \amp 1 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 1 \amp 1/5 \amp 1/5 \end{array} \right)\\ \amp \xrightarrow{R_1\to R_1-2R_2} \left( \begin{array}{cc|cc} 1 \amp 0 \amp 3/5 \amp -2/5 \\ 0 \amp 1 \amp 1/5 \amp 1/5 \end{array} \right) \end{align*}
    The inverse of \(A\) is the matrix
    \begin{equation*} A^{-1} = \begin{pmatrix} \frac{3}{5} \amp -\frac{2}{5} \\ \frac{1}{5} \amp \frac{1}{5}\end{pmatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    It can be readily checked that \(AA^{-1}=A^{-1}A=\Id_2\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.8.8.

    Consider the matrix \(A=\begin{pmatrix} 1 \amp 2 \amp -1 \\ 1 \amp 3 \amp 2 \\ 0 \amp 1 \amp 0\end{pmatrix}\text{.}\) The determinant of \(A\) is \(-3\text{.}\) Therefore, \(A\) is invertible. Let’s find its inverse.
    \begin{align*} \left( \begin{array}{ccc|ccc} 1 \amp 2 \amp -1 \amp 1 \amp 0 \amp 0 \\ 1 \amp 3 \amp 2 \amp 0 \amp 1 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 1 \amp 0 \amp 0 \amp 0 \amp 1 \end{array} \right)\amp\xrightarrow{R_2\to R_2-R_1}\left( \begin{array}{ccc|ccc} 1 \amp 2 \amp -1 \amp 1 \amp 0 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 1 \amp 3 \amp -1 \amp 1 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 1 \amp 0 \amp 0 \amp 0 \amp 1 \end{array} \right) \\ \amp\xrightarrow{R_3\to R_3-R_2}\left( \begin{array}{ccc|ccc} 1 \amp 2 \amp -1 \amp 1 \amp 0 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 1 \amp 3 \amp -1 \amp 1 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 0 \amp -3 \amp 1 \amp -1 \amp 1 \end{array} \right) \\ \amp \xrightarrow{R_3\to -R_3/3}\left( \begin{array}{ccc|ccc} 1 \amp 2 \amp -1 \amp 1 \amp 0 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 1 \amp 3 \amp -1 \amp 1 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 1 \amp 1/3 \amp -1/3 \amp 1/3 \end{array} \right)\\ \amp\xrightarrow{R_2\to R_2-3R_3}\left( \begin{array}{ccc|ccc} 1 \amp 2 \amp -1 \amp 1 \amp 0 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 1 \amp 0 \amp -2 \amp 2 \amp -1\\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 1 \amp 1/3 \amp -1/3 \amp 1/3 \end{array} \right) \\ \amp\xrightarrow{R_1\to R_1+R_3}\left( \begin{array}{ccc|ccc} 1 \amp 2 \amp 0 \amp 4/3 \amp -1/3 \amp 1/3 \\ 0 \amp 1 \amp 0 \amp -2 \amp 2 \amp -1\\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 1 \amp 1/3 \amp -1/3 \amp 1/3 \end{array} \right) \\ \amp\xrightarrow{R_1\to R_1-2R_2}\left( \begin{array}{ccc|ccc} 1 \amp 0 \amp 0 \amp 16/3 \amp -13/3 \amp 7/3 \\ 0 \amp 1 \amp 0 \amp -2 \amp 2 \amp -1\\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 1 \amp 1/3 \amp -1/3 \amp 1/3 \end{array} \right) \end{align*}
    So,
    \begin{equation*} A^{-1}=\begin{pmatrix}16/3 \amp -13/3 \amp 7/3 \\ -2 \amp 2 \amp -1\\ 1/3 \amp -1/3 \amp 1/3\end{pmatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    One nice application of invertible matrices to linear systems of equations is the following. If \(A\) is an invertible matrix with inverse \(A^{-1}\text{,}\) then the solution to the system \(A\bx=\bb\) can be easily obtained by multiplying both sides on the left by \(A^{-1}\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} \bx=A^{-1}A\bx=A^{-1}\bb\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗

    Example 2.8.9.

    Let’s consider the matrix \(A=\begin{pmatrix} 1 \amp 2 \\ -1 \amp 3\end{pmatrix}\) from Example 2.8.7. Consider the linear system given by
    \begin{equation*} A\colvec{x \\ y} = \colvec{1 \\ 3}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    We found in Example 2.8.7 that
    \begin{equation*} A^{-1}=\begin{pmatrix} \frac{3}{5} \amp -\frac{2}{5} \\ \frac{1}{5} \amp \frac{1}{5}\end{pmatrix}\text{,} \end{equation*}
    therefore, the solution to
    \begin{equation*} A\colvec{x \\ y} = \colvec{1 \\ 3} \end{equation*}
    is given by
    \begin{equation*} \colvec{x \\ y} = A^{-1}\colvec{1 \\ 3} = \begin{pmatrix} \frac{3}{5} \amp -\frac{2}{5} \\ \frac{1}{5} \amp \frac{1}{5}\end{pmatrix}\colvec{1 \\ 3}=\colvec{-3/5 \\ 4/5}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.8.10.

    If \(A=\begin{pmatrix}1 \amp 2 \amp -1 \\ 1 \amp 3 \amp 2 \\ 0 \amp 1 \amp 0\end{pmatrix}\text{,}\) we saw in Example 2.8.8 that its inverse is given by
    \begin{equation*} A^{-1}=\begin{pmatrix}16/3 \amp -13/3 \amp 7/3 \\ -2 \amp 2 \amp -1\\ 1/3 \amp -1/3 \amp 1/3\end{pmatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Therefore, if we wish to solve the linear system \(A\colvec{x \\ y \\ z}=\colvec{-1 \\ 1 \\ 1}\text{,}\) all we need to do is left-multiply the vector \(\colvec{- 1\\ 1 \\ 1}\) by the matrix \(A^{-1}\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} \colvec{x \\ y \\ z} = \begin{pmatrix}16/3 \amp -13/3 \amp 7/3 \\ -2 \amp 2 \amp -1\\ 1/3 \amp -1/3 \amp 1/3\end{pmatrix}\colvec{-1 \\ 1 \\ 1}=\colvec{-16/3 \\ 11/3 \\ -1/3}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    The last thing I would like to touch on in this chapter is the relationship between eigenvalues and the characteristic polynomial that I referred to in the previous section.
    🔗

    Theorem 2.8.11.

    Let \(A\) be a square matrix, and let \(p_A(x)\) be its characteristic polynomial. Then \(\lambda\in\R\) is an eigenvalue of \(A\) if and only if \(p_A(\lambda)=0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Recall that the characteristic polynomial of an \(n\times n\) matrix \(A\) is defined as \(p_A(x)=\det(x\Id_n-A)\text{.}\) So, if \(p_A(\lambda)=0\text{,}\) this means that \(\det(\lambda\Id_n-A)=0\text{.}\) Using Proposition 2.8.6, we conclude that the matrix \(\lambda\Id_n-A\) is not invertible. This means that its kernel contains non-zero vectors. So, if \(\bv\) is a non-zero vector of the kernel of \(\lambda\Id_n-A\text{,}\) we find \((\lambda\Id_n-A)\bv=\bz\text{.}\) Equivalently, \(A\bv=\lambda\bv\text{.}\) This implies that \(\lambda\) is an eigenvalue of \(A\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Conversely, let’s suppose that \(\lambda\) is an eigenvalue of \(A\text{.}\) This means that there is a non-zero vector \(\bv\in\R^n\) such that \(A\bv=\lambda\bv\text{.}\) This is equivalent to saying that there is a non-zero vector \(\bv\) such that \((\lambda\Id_n-A)\bv=0\text{.}\) Therefore, the kernel of \(\lambda\Id_n-A\) has dimension at least \(1\text{.}\) It follows that \(\lambda\Id_n-A\) is not invertible, and so, using Proposition 2.8.6 again, \(\det(\lambda\Id_n-A)=0\text{.}\)
    🔗
