    Section 2.6 Linear regression and the least squares method

    Linear regression is a method in mathematics, statistics and machine learning that aims to describe a linear relationship between a collection of independent variables and one dependent variable. The term regression originates from the 19th century study led by Francis Galton where he first observed the statistical phenomenon that came to be known as regression towards the mean. Galton found that certain some extreme characteristics are not fully passed from parents to their offspring. Instead, the offspring’s characteristic regress towards the mean. Using data measuring the height of parents and their children, Galton was able to quantify this regression for the case of height. We will be using Galton’s data to illustrate the process of linear regression. But before that, we will look at a small test case.
    🔗
    We will need the following result.
    🔗

    Proposition 2.6.1.

    Let \(V\) be a linear subspace of \(\R^n\text{,}\) and let \(\bv\) be a vector in \(\R^n\text{.}\) The vector in \(V\) that is the closest to \(\bv\) is \(\pr_V(\bv)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    We need to show that if \(\bw\) is any other vector in \(V\text{,}\) then
    \begin{equation*} \lVert\bv-\bw\rVert > \lVert\bv-\pr_V(\bv)\rVert\text{,} \end{equation*}
    or equivalently
    \begin{equation*} \lVert\bv-\bw\rVert^2 > \lVert\bv-\pr_V(\bv)\rVert^2\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Since we are assuming that \(\bw\) is a vector in \(V\) that is different from \(\pr_V(\bv)\text{,}\) we know that the vector \(\bu=\bw-\pr_V(\bv)\) lies in \(V\) and is non-zero. In particular, \(\lVert\bu\rVert > 0\text{.}\) Now,
    \begin{equation*} \lVert\bv-\bw\rVert^2 = (\bv-\bw)\cdot(\bv-\bw)= (\bv-\pr_V(\bv)-\bu)\cdot(\bv-\pr_V(\bv)-\bu).\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Expanding the right-hand side of this equation, we find that
    \begin{equation*} \lVert\bv-\bw\rVert^2 = (\bv-\pr_V(\bv))\cdot(\bv-\pr_V(\bv)) - 2\bu\cdot(\bv-\pr_V(\bv)) + \bu\cdot\bu\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Recalling that \(\bv-\pr_V(\bv)\) is orthogonal to \(V\text{,}\) we see that \(\bu\cdot(\bv-\pr_V(\bv))=0\text{.}\) But then
    \begin{equation*} \lVert\bv-\bw\rVert^2 = \lVert\bv-\pr_V(\bv)\rVert^2 + \lVert\bu\rVert^2\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Since \(\lVert\bu\rVert > 0\text{,}\) we conclude that \(\lVert\bv-\bw\rVert^2 > \lVert\bv-\pr_V(\bv)\rVert^2\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    Let’s suppose that we have a collection of \(m\) data points
    \begin{equation*} \bp_1=(x_{11}, x_{12},\ldots,x_{1n}, y_1),\ldots, \bp_m=(x_{m1},x_{m2},\ldots,x_{mn}, y_m) \end{equation*}
    and that our aim is to explain the values \(y_i\) in terms of \(x_{ij}\text{,}\) \(1\leq j\leq n\text{.}\) The first \(n\) entries of the data points \(\bp_i\) are then called the independent variables, while the last one is called the dependent variable. The easiest kind of relation that these variables could satisfy is a linear relationship. We say that the relationship between the independent variables and the dependent one is linear if there are \(n+1\) scalars \(a_0, a_1,\ldots, a_n\in\R\) such that the value
    \begin{equation*} a_0 + a_1x_{i1} + a_2x_{i2} + \ldots + a_nx_{in} \end{equation*}
    is a good approximation of the value \(y_i\) for every \(i\) between \(1\) and \(m\text{.}\) Our task is to find the coefficients \(a_0, a_1,\ldots,a_n\) that best approximate the values \(y_i\text{,}\) in the sense that they minimize the sum of square residues
    \begin{equation*} | a_0 + a_1x_{11} + a_2x_{12} + \ldots + a_nx_{1n}-y_1|^2 + \ldots + |a_0 + a_1x_{m1} + a_2x_{m2} + \ldots + a_nx_{mn} - y_m|^2\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    It will be useful to separate the values in our data points corresponding to independent variables from those corresponding to dependent variables. So let \(\by\) be the vector in \(\R^m\) whose \(i\)th entry is \(y_i\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} \by=\colvec{y_1 \\ y_2 \\ \vdots \\ y_m}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    The independent variables will all be organized in a single \(m\times (n+1)\) matrix \(X\) where row \(i\) consists of the vector \((1,x_{11}, x_{12},\ldots,x_{1n})\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} X=\begin{pmatrix} 1 & x_{11} & x_{12} & \cdots & x_{1n} \\ 1 & x_{21} & x_{22} & \cdots & x_{2n} \\ \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ 1 & x_{m1} & x_{m2} & \cdots & x_{mn}\end{pmatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Using the vector \(\by\) and the matrix \(X\text{,}\) we can rewrite the square residue minimization condition in the following form: we are trying to find the vector \(\ba=\colvec{a_0\\a_1\\ \vdots\\a_n}\) in \(\R^{n+1}\) that minimizes the distance between \(X\ba\) and \(\by\text{.}\)
    🔗
    This is where projections come in handy. What we are trying to do is to find the closest vector in \(V=\Img(X)\) to \(\by\text{.}\) Proposition 2.6.1 tells us that that vector is precisely \(\pr_V(\by)\text{.}\) Therefore, we want to solve
    \begin{equation*} X\ba=\pr_V(\by)\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗

    Proposition 2.6.2.

    The vector \(\bv\in\R^{n+1}\) is a solution to \(X\ba=\pr_V(\by)\) if and only if it is a solution to \(X^TX\ba=X^T\by\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Suppose that \(\bv\) is a solution to \(X\ba=\pr_V(\by)\text{.}\) Then we have
    \begin{equation*} X^TX\bv = X^T\pr_V(\by)\text{.} \end{equation*}
    We know that \(\bu=\by-\pr_V(\by)\) is orthogonal to \(V\text{.}\) Exercise 2.5.8 states that the kernel of \(X^T\) is the orthogonal complement of \(\Img(X)\) in \(\R^m\text{.}\) Since \(\bu\) is in the orthogonal complement of \(\Img(X)\text{,}\) we conclude that \(X^T\bu=\bz\text{.}\) But this means that \(X^T\by=X^T\pr_V(\by)\text{.}\) So, \(X^TX\bv=X^T\pr_V(\by)=X^T\by\text{.}\)
    🔗
    We now prove the converse. Suppose that \(\bv\) is a solution to \(X^TX\ba=X^T\by\text{.}\) Rewriting the equation, we get
    \begin{equation*} X^T(X\ba-\by)=\bz\text{.} \end{equation*}
    In other words, \(X\ba-\by\) is in the kernel of \(X^T\text{.}\) Since the kernel of \(X^T\) is \(\Img(X)^{\perp}\text{,}\) this means that \(X\ba-\by\) is a vector in \(\Img(X)\text{.}\) Again, writing \(\by=\bu+\pr_V(\by)\text{,}\) and using the fact that \(\bu\in\Img(X)^{\perp}\text{,}\) we conclude that \(X\ba-\pr_V(\by)\) is in \(\Img(X)^{\perp}\text{.}\) However, \(X\ba\) and \(\pr_V(\by)\) are two vectors in \(\Img(X)\text{,}\) and so \(X\ba-\pr_V(\by)\in \Img(X)\cap\Img(X)^{\perp}\text{.}\) Therefore, \(X\ba-\pr_V(\by)=\bz\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.6.3.

    Consider the points \((1,2)\text{,}\) \((2,5)\) and \((4,7)\) in \(\R^2\text{.}\) Let’s try to find the line of best fit, by which I mean the line \(y=mx+b\) that minimizes
    \begin{equation*} |m+b-1|^2+|2m+b-5|^2+|4m+b-7|^2\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    The values in the second entry of the vectors above correspond to the values of the dependent variable, so \(\by=\colvec{2 \\ 5 \\ 7}\) and
    \begin{equation*} X=\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 1 & 2 \\ 1 & 4\end{pmatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    The transpose is given by
    \begin{equation*} X^T=\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 & 1 \\ 1 & 2 & 4\end{pmatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    The product \(X^TX\) is given by
    \begin{equation*} X^TX=\begin{pmatrix} 3 & 7 \\ 7 & 21 \end{pmatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    The linear system that we need to solve is
    \begin{equation*} \begin{pmatrix} 3 & 7 \\ 7 & 21 \end{pmatrix}\colvec{b \\ m}=\colvec{14 \\ 40}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    The solution is
    \begin{equation*} \colvec{b \\ m}=\colvec{1 \\ \frac{11}{7}}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    So the line of best fit for this set of three points is \(y=\frac{11}{7}x+1\text{.}\)
